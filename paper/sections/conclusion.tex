\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have introduced \ecagbench{}, a benchmark of 332 constructive problems in algebraic geometry designed to evaluate a mode of mathematical reasoning---object construction---that is central to mathematical practice but underrepresented in existing LLM benchmarks.

\paragraph{Key findings.}
Our pilot evaluation of Claude Opus~4.6 and Claude Sonnet~4.6 on a stratified 100-problem subset reveals:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{A steep difficulty gradient:} Opus achieves 90\% overall accuracy but drops from 95\% (Level~1) to 64\% (Level~3), a 31-percentage-point decline. Sonnet shows an even steeper drop: 95\% to 27\%.
  \item \textbf{Counterexamples are harder than examples:} Both models show a persistent gap (Opus: ${\sim}$9pp, Sonnet: ${\sim}$15pp), confirming that understanding theorem boundaries requires deeper reasoning than applying theorems directly.
  \item \textbf{Scale benefits deep reasoning disproportionately:} Opus outperforms Sonnet by 12pp overall, but the gap is 0pp at Level~1, 12pp at Level~2, and 36pp at Level~3. The largest per-chapter gain is on Moduli ($+$24pp).
  \item \textbf{Timeouts as a failure mode:} Sonnet times out on 18\% of problems (45.5\% at Level~3) vs.\ Opus's 4\%, suggesting that model scale helps avoid ``silent failures'' where no response is generated.
  \item \textbf{Failure modes are structured:} Property confusion (F2) is the most common failure among completed responses, accounting for 50\% of Opus's classified errors.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our evaluation has several limitations:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Same-family models:} Both models are from the Claude family. Cross-family comparison (GPT-4o, Gemini 2.5 Pro, DeepSeek-R1~\citep{deepseek2025r1}) is needed to assess generalizability.
  \item \textbf{Hybrid scoring limitations:} Although the LLM judge scores against human-authored reference solutions (not just answer keys), the judge is from the same model family as the solver, introducing potential concordance bias. The reference solutions themselves may contain minor errors. Independent human expert scoring is needed to fully validate these results (Section~\ref{sec:self-eval-limitations}).
  \item \textbf{Single-turn only:} Multi-turn interaction with CAS feedback may substantially improve performance.
  \item \textbf{Domain specificity:} Results in algebraic geometry may not generalize to other mathematical domains.
  \item \textbf{CAS coverage:} While 93.1\% of problems use CAS verification, some verifications require specialized scripts not yet implemented.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Future work.}
We plan several extensions:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Human expert scoring:} Full human review of all 100 responses to establish unbiased baselines and validate the automated scoring.
  \item \textbf{Cross-family evaluation:} Evaluate GPT-4o, Gemini~2.5~Pro, DeepSeek-R1, and Llemma \citep{azerbayev2024llemma} to determine which patterns are model-specific vs.\ fundamental.
  \item \textbf{Full-scale evaluation:} Evaluate all 332 problems with automated CAS verification scripts.
  \item \textbf{Multi-turn interaction:} Enable models to refine constructions based on CAS feedback.
  \item \textbf{Lean~4 integration:} Formalize a subset of problems for formal verification.
  \item \textbf{Fine-tuning studies:} Investigate whether training on algebraic geometry textbooks \citep{hartshorne1977algebraic, vakil2017rising, eisenbud1995commutative} improves constructive reasoning.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Broader impact.}
Constructive mathematical reasoning is a prerequisite for using LLMs as research tools in mathematics.
The pronounced difficulty of counterexample construction is particularly significant: as Lakatos~\citep{lakatos1976proofs} argued, mathematical progress depends on the ability to find refutations.
Our cross-model comparison shows that this ability improves with scale---the counterexample gap narrows from ${\sim}$15pp (Sonnet) to ${\sim}$9pp (Opus)---suggesting that future, more capable models may further close this gap.
\ecagbench{} provides a concrete measure of progress.
