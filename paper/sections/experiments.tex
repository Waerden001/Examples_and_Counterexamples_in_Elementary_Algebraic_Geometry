\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We report results from a pilot evaluation designed to establish initial baselines, validate the benchmark design, and investigate the example-versus-counterexample performance gap.

\subsection{Evaluation Setup}

\paragraph{Model.}
We evaluate Claude Opus~4~\citep{anthropic2024claude}, a frontier language model from Anthropic, using the API with temperature~0 and a maximum output length of 4{,}096 tokens.
We focus on a single strong model to enable deep qualitative analysis rather than superficial comparison across many models; multi-model evaluation is planned for future work.

\paragraph{Subset selection.}
From the full 332-problem benchmark, we select a stratified random subset of 50 problems.
The stratification ensures proportional representation across chapter, difficulty level, and question type, with a minimum of 12 counterexample problems to provide statistical power for the examples-versus-counterexamples comparison.
The resulting subset contains 36 examples, 12 counterexamples, 1 computation, and 1 proof, distributed as: 24 Basic Concepts, 10 Computations, 9 Moduli, 7 Cohomology; and 12 Level~1, 35 Level~2, 3 Level~3.

\paragraph{Prompt.}
Each problem is presented using the prompt template described in Section~\ref{sec:benchmark} (full prompt in Appendix~\ref{app:prompt}).

\paragraph{Scoring.}
All 50 responses were scored by a human expert in algebraic geometry using the 0/0.5/1 scale.
Each incorrect or partially correct response was additionally tagged with a failure mode (F1--F6, defined in Section~\ref{sec:analysis}).

\subsection{Case Studies}

We present three case studies that illustrate representative model behaviors, from successful construction to characteristic failure modes.

\paragraph{Case Study 1: Successful Level~1 construction.}
\ecagid{0024} asks whether the scheme $\coprod_{i=1}^{\infty} \Spec(k)$ is affine, and to identify which property fails.
The model correctly identified that this is a counterexample: an infinite disjoint union of affine schemes need not be affine.
It correctly noted that $\coprod_{i=1}^{\infty} \Spec(k) = \Spec(\prod_{i=1}^{\infty} k)$ if it were affine, but $\prod_{i=1}^{\infty} k$ has the wrong spectrum (it is not discrete), and it correctly cited that affine schemes are quasi-compact while infinite disjoint unions are not.
This demonstrates the model's ability to handle foundational counterexamples where the relevant property (quasi-compactness) is well-known.

\paragraph{Case Study 2: Counterexample failure (F2 -- property confusion).}
Consider a problem asking the model to exhibit a reduced scheme over $\mathbf{F}_p(T)$ that is not geometrically reduced.
The model correctly identified the key issue (purely inseparable extensions create nilpotents upon base change) and proposed $\Spec(\mathbf{F}_p(T^{1/p}))$ as the scheme.
However, in the verification step, the model confused the property by attempting to show that $\Spec(\mathbf{F}_p(T^{1/p}))$ is itself non-reduced (which it is not---it is a field!) rather than showing that its base change $\Spec(\mathbf{F}_p(T^{1/p}) \otimes_{\mathbf{F}_p(T)} \mathbf{F}_p(T^{1/p}))$ is non-reduced.
The construction was correct but the verification addressed the wrong property, a hallmark of F2 failures.

\paragraph{Case Study 3: Deep problem failure (F5 -- hallucinated theorem).}
On a Level~3 problem about constructing a smooth projective variety with specific Hodge numbers, the model produced a lengthy response invoking a ``generalized Lefschetz hyperplane theorem for Hodge filtrations'' that does not exist in the stated form.
The claimed variety was described using this non-existent result, and no explicit equations or constructions were given.
This exemplifies F5 (hallucinated theorem): when the model lacks the specific knowledge to construct a deep example, it fabricates a plausible-sounding theoretical justification rather than admitting uncertainty.

\subsection{Comparison with Existing Benchmarks}

Direct comparison with other benchmarks is difficult due to differences in domain and evaluation methodology.
However, we note that the 34\% accuracy on \ecagbench{} is broadly consistent with reported difficulties of frontier models on research-level mathematics:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item FrontierMath~\citep{glazer2024frontiermath} reported that frontier models solve fewer than 2\% of their research-level problems, though their problems are designed to be at the frontier of mathematical knowledge.
  \item On the MATH benchmark~\citep{hendrycks2021measuring}, frontier models now achieve 90+\% accuracy, indicating that competition-level mathematics is largely solved.
  \item On PutnamBench~\citep{tsoukalas2024putnambench}, formal theorem proving success rates remain low for the hardest problems.
\end{itemize}

\ecagbench{} occupies an intermediate position: harder than competition math but more accessible than frontier research, targeting the ``working knowledge'' level of a graduate student in algebraic geometry.
The 34\% accuracy suggests that current models have significant room for improvement in this regime.
