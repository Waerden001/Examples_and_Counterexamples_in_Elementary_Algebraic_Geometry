\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

We analyze the pilot results in depth, focusing on the Opus~4.6 evaluation as the primary dataset (96 completed responses, 4 timeouts) with Sonnet~4.6 comparisons where informative.

\subsection{Failure Mode Taxonomy}

We define six failure modes specific to constructive algebraic geometry reasoning.
Table~\ref{tab:failure-modes} summarizes their frequency among Opus's non-perfect responses.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Distribution of failure modes in the Opus~4.6 pilot evaluation (10 incorrect or partial responses out of 100). Timeouts (4) are excluded as no response is available for classification.}
\label{tab:failure-modes}
\begin{tabular}{llcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Code} & \textbf{Failure Mode} & \textbf{Count} & \textbf{\% of errors} \\
\midrule
F2 & Property confusion & 3 & 50\% \\
F3 & Computational errors & 1 & 17\% \\
F5 & Hallucinated theorems & 1 & 17\% \\
F6 & Incomplete verification & 1 & 17\% \\
\midrule
\multicolumn{2}{l}{Total classified} & 6 & 100\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{F1: Fabricated objects.}
The model produces a mathematical object that does not exist or is not well-defined.
No F1 errors were observed in the Opus pilot, suggesting that Opus rarely invents non-existent algebraic structures.

\paragraph{F2: Property confusion (50\% of errors).}
The most common failure mode.
The model constructs a valid object but confuses the required properties.
\emph{Example (\ecagid{0174}):} Asked to compute the class group of a specific scheme, the model produced a largely correct analysis but confused divisor class relations in the final step, yielding the wrong group.
The construction strategy was sound, but the model failed to track which divisors generate the class group.

Property confusion was also the dominant failure mode in the Sonnet evaluation, consistent with its being a fundamental limitation of current LLMs on constructive mathematics.

\paragraph{F3: Computational errors (17\%).}
The model follows a correct strategy but makes arithmetic or algebraic errors.
\emph{Example (\ecagid{0256}):} In computing $\Pic(X)$ for a specific variety, the model made an arithmetic error in an intermediate step that happened to cancel, yielding the correct final answer but with flawed reasoning.
This class of errors is insidious because the narrative appears correct and only careful verification reveals the mistake.

\paragraph{F4: Trivial or degenerate answers.}
Not observed in the Opus pilot. This failure mode (producing trivially degenerate examples) may be suppressed by the prompt's explicit instruction to provide non-trivial constructions.

\paragraph{F5: Hallucinated theorems (17\%).}
The model invokes a non-existent result.
\emph{Example (\ecagid{0002}):} In a multi-part problem involving Hensel's lemma and Zariski density of $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$, the model handled the first parts correctly but invoked a version of strong approximation beyond what the problem setup warranted, receiving partial credit.

\paragraph{F6: Incomplete verification (17\%).}
The model constructs a plausible object but fails to verify all required properties.
\emph{Example (\ecagid{0090}):} On a Level~3 problem about Hironaka's construction, the model correctly set up the two blow-up orderings and the gluing construction, but did not fully verify that the resulting variety has the claimed pathological properties.

\subsection{Difficulty Calibration}
\label{sec:difficulty-calibration}

Both models show a clear monotonic relationship between assigned difficulty and accuracy:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
& & Level 1 & Level 2 & Level 3 \\
\midrule
\multirow{3}{*}{Opus} & Accuracy & 0.95 & 0.93 & 0.64 \\
& Avg.\ score & 0.98 & 0.96 & 0.68 \\
& Timeout rate & 0\% & 1.5\% & 27.3\% \\
\midrule
\multirow{3}{*}{Sonnet} & Accuracy & 0.95 & 0.81 & 0.27 \\
& Avg.\ score & 0.95 & 0.81 & 0.41 \\
& Timeout rate & 4.5\% & 17.9\% & 45.5\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

This validates the difficulty calibration: problems rated harder by a human expert are indeed harder for both models.
For Opus, the steepest drop is from Level~2 to Level~3 (29 percentage points), corresponding to the transition from problems requiring a single non-trivial theorem to those requiring deep synthesis of multiple advanced results.
Notably, Opus maintains near-ceiling performance through Level~2, with the difficulty curve only ``bending'' at Level~3.

The timeout rate provides an important complementary signal.
At Level~3, Opus's 27\% timeout rate (3/11) is still substantial but much lower than Sonnet's 45.5\% (5/11), indicating that model scale helps the model at least \emph{attempt} deep problems rather than failing silently.

\subsection{Chapter-Level Analysis}
\label{sec:chapter-analysis}

Performance varies across chapters for both models:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Chapter} & \textbf{$n$} & \textbf{Opus Acc.} & \textbf{Sonnet Acc.} & $\Delta$ \\
\midrule
Cohomology & 15 & 1.00 & 0.93 & $+$7pp \\
Basic Concepts & 47 & 0.91 & 0.79 & $+$13pp \\
Moduli & 17 & 0.88 & 0.65 & $+$24pp \\
Computations & 21 & 0.81 & 0.76 & $+$5pp \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Several patterns emerge:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Cohomology} achieves perfect accuracy for Opus, reflecting the structured, algorithm-like nature of cohomological computations (spectral sequences, long exact sequences).
  \item \textbf{Moduli} shows the largest cross-model improvement ($+$24pp), suggesting that reasoning about abstract moduli constructions benefits substantially from model scale.
  \item \textbf{Computations} shows the smallest improvement ($+$5pp) and the lowest Opus accuracy (81\%), confirming that multi-step algebraic calculations remain challenging regardless of scale.
  Both partial-credit responses in this chapter involved arithmetic errors (F3) in otherwise correct approaches.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Example vs.\ Counterexample Performance Gap}
\label{sec:gap-analysis}

Both models show a gap between example and counterexample accuracy:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
& \textbf{Example} & \textbf{Counterexample} & \textbf{Gap} \\
\midrule
Opus & 91.4\% & 82.6\% & 8.8pp \\
Sonnet & 80\% & 65\% & 15pp \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The gap persists across both models but narrows with scale (from 15pp to 9pp (approximate)), suggesting that larger models are better at the ``boundary awareness'' required for counterexample construction---understanding not just where a theorem applies but precisely where its hypotheses fail.

Among Opus's 6 partial-credit responses, 2 were on counterexample problems.
Among Sonnet's 4 partial-credit responses, 3 were on counterexamples.
This pattern---counterexamples receiving more partial credit---suggests that counterexample construction often yields partially correct constructions where the right mathematical territory is identified but precise execution fails.

This finding is consistent with the Lakatosian view~\citep{lakatos1976proofs} that counterexample construction requires ``concept stretching''---pushing a definition to its limits to find where it breaks.
The narrowing of the gap with model scale suggests that this ability, while not fully achieved, is learnable.

\subsection{Timeout Analysis}
\label{sec:timeout-analysis}

Timeouts---problems where the model produced no response within 600 seconds---reveal a qualitatively distinct failure mode.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
& \textbf{Opus} & \textbf{Sonnet} \\
\midrule
Total timeouts & 4/100 (4\%) & 18/100 (18\%) \\
Level~1 & 0/22 (0\%) & 1/22 (4.5\%) \\
Level~2 & 1/67 (1.5\%) & 12/67 (17.9\%) \\
Level~3 & 3/11 (27.3\%) & 5/11 (45.5\%) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Several observations:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Sonnet's 18\% timeout rate contributes significantly to its lower overall accuracy. Among Sonnet's non-timeout responses, accuracy is 95.1\% (78/82), comparable to Opus's 93.8\% (90/96) among non-timeout responses.
  \item The 2 problems that both models timed out on (\ecagid{0362}, \ecagid{0364}) are both from Computations, suggesting that certain multi-step algebraic calculations exceed both models' generation capabilities regardless of scale.
  \item Of the 16 problems where only Sonnet timed out, Opus scored 1.0 on 12 and 0.5 on 4, demonstrating that model scale substantially reduces the ``silent failure'' rate while maintaining high quality on the recovered problems.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations of LLM-Based Scoring}
\label{sec:self-eval-limitations}

A significant limitation is that the automated scorer is an LLM from the same family as the solver.
Among Opus's 96 non-timeout responses, 90 (93.8\%) were scored as correct, a rate that may reflect concordance bias.

Several factors warrant caution:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item The scorer may share the solver's blindspots, rating as correct constructions that a domain expert would flag.
  \item The scorer was given the known solution, which may bias it toward confirming structurally similar responses.
  \item With only 6 classified failures among 96 Opus responses, the failure mode taxonomy remains sparsely populated.
\end{itemize}

Future work will address this through human expert scoring of the full 100-problem subset, cross-family evaluation (e.g., using GPT-4o to score Claude responses), and expanded CAS-based automated verification.
