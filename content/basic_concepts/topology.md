### Example: \'{E}tale fundamental group of the nodal curve $y^{2}=x^{2}(x+1)$ {#ecag-0179}

**Statement:** We compute the etale fundamental group of the nodal cubic curve $C$ defined by $y^2 = x^2(x+1)$ over an algebraically closed field $k$ of characteristic $\neq 2$. The result is that $\pi_1^{\text{et}}(C, \overline{c}) \cong \widehat{\mathbb{Z}}$, the profinite completion of $\mathbb{Z}$.

**Construction/Proof:** Consider the curve $C \subset \mathbb{A}^2_k$ defined by $y^2 = x^2(x+1)$. This curve has a node (ordinary double point) at the origin $(0,0)$, since both partial derivatives vanish there:

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2(x+1) - y^2) = 3x^2 + 2x = 0 \quad \text{at } (0,0),

$$

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2(x+1) - y^2) = -2y = 0 \quad \text{at } (0,0).

$$

The Hessian matrix at the origin has eigenvalues corresponding to two distinct tangent directions (since $\operatorname{char} k \neq 2$), confirming the node.

**Step 1: Normalization.** The normalization $\widetilde{C} \to C$ resolves the node. Parametrically, set $y = tx$, so $t^2 x^2 = x^2(x+1)$, giving $x = t^2 - 1$ and $y = t(t^2 - 1)$. This gives a map $\mathbb{A}^1_k \to C$ which is the normalization. The normalization $\widetilde{C} \cong \mathbb{A}^1_k$ maps to $C$, and the preimage of the node $(0,0)$ consists of two points $t = 1$ and $t = -1$.

**Step 2: The fundamental group via the normalization sequence.** There is a pushout diagram in the category of schemes: $C$ is obtained from $\widetilde{C} \cong \mathbb{A}^1_k$ by identifying the two points $t = 1$ and $t = -1$. By the van Kampen theorem for etale fundamental groups (SGA 1, Expose IX), we have a corresponding exact/amalgamation sequence. Since $\mathbb{A}^1_k$ over an algebraically closed field of characteristic zero is simply connected in the etale sense ($\pi_1^{\text{et}}(\mathbb{A}^1_k) = 0$), and since we are identifying two points, the fundamental group of the nodal curve is generated by a loop around the node.

**Step 3: Finite etale covers.** More concretely, every finite etale cover of $C$ is obtained by unwinding the identification at the node. The finite etale covers of $C$ correspond to finite etale covers of $\mathbb{A}^1_k$ together with compatible identifications at the two preimage points. Since $\pi_1^{\text{et}}(\mathbb{A}^1_k) = 0$ (in characteristic zero), the only etale covers of $\mathbb{A}^1_k$ are trivial, so the covers of $C$ are classified by the gluing data at the node. This gluing is given by a permutation action of $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ on the $n$ sheets over the node, yielding $\pi_1^{\text{et}}(C) \cong \widehat{\mathbb{Z}}$.

In positive characteristic $p \neq 2$, one must also account for Artin--Schreier covers of $\mathbb{A}^1_k$, and the result is that $\pi_1^{\text{et}}(C)$ is larger, but the tame part remains $\widehat{\mathbb{Z}}^{(p')}$ (the prime-to-$p$ profinite completion).

**Key Insight:** The etale fundamental group of a nodal curve behaves analogously to the topological fundamental group of a pinched torus: the node creates a loop, contributing a copy of $\widehat{\mathbb{Z}}$. This illustrates how singularities enrich the fundamental group compared to the normalization.

**Prerequisites:** Etale fundamental group, normalization of curves, nodal singularities, van Kampen theorem for etale fundamental groups, Artin--Schreier covers

### Example: Hartshorne $\mathrm{III}.10.5$, \'{e}tale locally free implies Zariski locally free {#ecag-0180}

**Statement:** Let $X$ be a Noetherian scheme and $\mathscr{F}$ a coherent sheaf on $X$. If $\mathscr{F}$ becomes free when pulled back along an etale cover $f: U \to X$ (i.e., $f^*\mathscr{F} \cong \mathcal{O}_U^r$), then $\mathscr{F}$ is already locally free in the Zariski topology.

**Construction/Proof:** Suppose $f: U \to X$ is a faithfully flat and etale morphism such that $f^*\mathscr{F} \cong \mathcal{O}_U^r$. We need to show $\mathscr{F}$ is locally free on $X$ in the Zariski topology.

**Step 1: Choosing generators.** Since $\mathscr{F}$ is coherent, at any point $x \in X$ we can choose $r$ local sections $s_1, \ldots, s_r$ of $\mathscr{F}$ near $x$ that generate $\mathscr{F}_x / \mathfrak{m}_x \mathscr{F}_x \cong k(x)^r$ (using that $f^*\mathscr{F}$ has rank $r$, so $\mathscr{F}$ has constant rank $r$ near $x$). These sections define a surjection

$$
\varphi: \mathcal{O}_{X}^{r} \to \mathscr{F} \to 0

$$

in a Zariski neighborhood of $x$, by Nakayama's lemma (surjectivity can be checked on the fiber).

**Step 2: Pulling back and using flatness.** Let $K = \ker(\varphi)$, so we have an exact sequence

$$
0 \to K \to \mathcal{O}_{X}^{r} \to \mathscr{F} \to 0.

$$

Since $f$ is flat, pulling back preserves exactness:

$$
0 \to f^{*}K \to \mathcal{O}_{U}^{r} \to f^{*}\mathscr{F} \to 0.

$$

But $f^*\mathscr{F} \cong \mathcal{O}_U^r$ by hypothesis, so the surjection $\mathcal{O}_U^r \to \mathcal{O}_U^r$ is a map between free modules of the same rank. Note: we only need flatness of $f$ for this step, not the full etale condition.

**Step 3: Applying Nakayama's lemma to the kernel.** At any point $u \in U$ lying over $x$, tensor the pulled-back sequence with $k(u)$:

$$
f^{*}K \otimes k(u) \to k(u)^{r} \to f^{*}\mathscr{F} \otimes k(u) \to 0.

$$

Since $f^*\mathscr{F} \cong \mathcal{O}_U^r$, we have $f^*\mathscr{F} \otimes k(u) \cong k(u)^r$, so the map $k(u)^r \to k(u)^r$ is a surjection between $k(u)$-vector spaces of the same dimension, hence an isomorphism. Therefore $f^*K \otimes k(u) = 0$. By Nakayama's lemma (applied to the finitely generated module $(f^*K)_u$ over the local ring $\mathcal{O}_{U,u}$), we conclude $(f^*K)_u = 0$.

**Step 4: Descending to $X$.** Since this holds at all points $u \in U$, we have $f^*K = 0$. Now, $f$ is faithfully flat (etale and surjective), so $f^*K = 0$ implies $K = 0$ by faithful flatness. Hence $\varphi: \mathcal{O}_X^r \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathscr{F}$ is an isomorphism in a Zariski neighborhood of $x$.

Since $x$ was arbitrary, $\mathscr{F}$ is Zariski locally free.

**Key Insight:** The proof uses two key ingredients: (1) flatness of the etale map to pull back exact sequences, and (2) Nakayama's lemma to conclude that a finitely generated module whose fiber vanishes must itself vanish. The etale condition is used primarily to ensure faithful flatness (flat + surjective), which allows descent of the vanishing of the kernel from $U$ to $X$.

**Prerequisites:** Coherent sheaves, Nakayama's lemma, flat morphisms, etale morphisms, faithful flatness and descent

### Example: $\mathbb{A}^{1}$ is not algebraically simply connected {#ecag-0181}

**Statement:** Over a field $k$ of characteristic $p > 0$, the affine line $\mathbb{A}^1_k$ is not algebraically simply connected, i.e., $\pi_1^{\text{et}}(\mathbb{A}^1_k, \overline{a}) \neq 0$. In fact, the Artin--Schreier map provides a non-trivial connected finite etale cover of $\mathbb{A}^1_k$.

**Construction/Proof:** Let $k$ be an algebraically closed field of characteristic $p > 0$. Consider the Artin--Schreier morphism

$$
\varphi: \mathbb{A}^1_k \to \mathbb{A}^1_k, \quad t \mapsto t^p - t.

$$

**Step 1: $\varphi$ is etale.** The map $\varphi$ corresponds to the ring map $k[x] \to k[t]$ sending $x \mapsto t^p - t$. The derivative of $t^p - t$ with respect to $t$ is $pt^{p-1} - 1 = -1$ (since $\operatorname{char} k = p$), which is a unit everywhere. Hence $\varphi$ is etale.

**Step 2: $\varphi$ is finite.** The extension $k(t)/k(t^p - t)$ is a degree-$p$ separable extension (separable because $\varphi$ is etale). Thus $\varphi$ is finite of degree $p$.

**Step 3: $\varphi$ is a non-trivial connected cover.** The fiber over any point $x = a \in k$ consists of the $p$ roots of $t^p - t = a$ in $k$, which are $\{t_0, t_0 + 1, t_0 + 2, \ldots, t_0 + (p-1)\}$ for any root $t_0$ (since if $t_0^p - t_0 = a$, then $(t_0 + c)^p - (t_0 + c) = t_0^p + c^p - t_0 - c = a + c^p - c = a$ when $c \in \mathbb{F}_p$). Since $k$ is algebraically closed, all fibers have exactly $p$ points, confirming $\varphi$ is a finite etale cover of degree $p$.

The cover is connected because $k[t]$ is a domain, and the Galois group is $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ acting by $t \mapsto t + 1$.

**Step 4: Consequence.** Since there exists a non-trivial connected finite etale cover $\varphi: \mathbb{A}^1_k \to \mathbb{A}^1_k$ of degree $p > 1$, the etale fundamental group $\pi_1^{\text{et}}(\mathbb{A}^1_k)$ is non-trivial.

In fact, the full structure of $\pi_1^{\text{et}}(\mathbb{A}^1_k)$ in characteristic $p$ is enormously complicated. Abhyankar's conjecture (proved by Raynaud and Harbater) characterizes which finite groups occur as Galois groups of etale covers of $\mathbb{A}^1_k$: a finite group $G$ is a quotient of $\pi_1^{\text{et}}(\mathbb{A}^1_k)$ if and only if $G$ is quasi-$p$, meaning $G$ is generated by its $p$-Sylow subgroups.

**Key Insight:** In characteristic $p > 0$, the Frobenius endomorphism and the additive group structure of $\mathbb{A}^1$ produce Artin--Schreier covers $t \mapsto t^p - t$ that are finite etale of degree $p$, with no analogue in characteristic zero. This is the fundamental reason $\mathbb{A}^1$ fails to be simply connected in positive characteristic.

**Prerequisites:** Etale fundamental group, Artin--Schreier extensions, characteristic $p$ algebraic geometry, Abhyankar's conjecture

### Remark: $\mathbb{P}_{K}^{1}$ is algebraically simply connected {#ecag-0182}

When $K$ is a separably closed field, $\mathbb{P}^1_K$ is algebraically simply connected, meaning $\pi_1^{\text{et}}(\mathbb{P}^1_K, \overline{x}) = 0$. This is a consequence of the Riemann--Hurwitz formula: if $f: C \to \mathbb{P}^1_K$ is a finite etale cover of degree $d$, then $C$ is a smooth projective curve of genus $g(C)$. The Riemann--Hurwitz formula gives

$$
2g(C) - 2 = d(2 \cdot 0 - 2) + \deg R = -2d + \deg R

$$

where $R$ is the ramification divisor. Since $f$ is etale, $\deg R = 0$, so $2g(C) - 2 = -2d$, hence $g(C) = 1 - d$. Since $g(C) \geq 0$, we must have $d \leq 1$, so $d = 1$ and $f$ is an isomorphism.

The hypothesis that $K$ is separably closed is essential. Over a non-separably-closed field $K$, there exist non-trivial finite etale covers of $\mathbb{P}^1_K$ arising from the arithmetic fundamental group. For instance, if $K$ is a number field, the absolute Galois group $\operatorname{Gal}(\overline{K}/K)$ contributes a non-trivial geometric part to $\pi_1^{\text{et}}(\mathbb{P}^1_K)$ via the exact sequence

$$
1 \to \pi_1^{\text{et}}(\mathbb{P}^1_{\overline{K}}) \to \pi_1^{\text{et}}(\mathbb{P}^1_K) \to \operatorname{Gal}(\overline{K}/K) \to 1.

$$

Since $\pi_1^{\text{et}}(\mathbb{P}^1_{\overline{K}}) = 0$ (by the argument above, as $\overline{K}$ is algebraically closed, hence separably closed), this gives $\pi_1^{\text{et}}(\mathbb{P}^1_K) \cong \operatorname{Gal}(\overline{K}/K)$, which is highly non-trivial.

For a comprehensive treatment, see Lenstra's notes [Galois theory for schemes](http://websites.math.leidenuniv.nl/algebra/GSchemes.pdf).

### Remark: Sheaf cohomology, Zariski topology,  étale topology {#ecag-0183}

The choice of topology on a scheme profoundly affects sheaf cohomology. The following key facts illustrate the difference between Zariski and etale cohomology:

**1. Grothendieck vanishing and constant sheaves.** The Grothendieck vanishing theorem states that for a Noetherian topological space $X$ of dimension $n$, $H^i_{\operatorname{Zar}}(X, \mathscr{F}) = 0$ for all $i > n$ and any sheaf of abelian groups $\mathscr{F}$ on $X$ (in the Zariski topology). Since $\mathbb{P}^1$ has Zariski dimension 1, this gives

$$
H^2_{\operatorname{Zar}}(\mathbb{P}^1, \underline{k}) = 0

$$

where $\underline{k}$ denotes the constant sheaf associated to the field $k$. However, in the etale topology, the "dimension" relevant for vanishing is the etale cohomological dimension, which is different. In fact,

$$
H^2_{\text{et}}(\mathbb{P}^1, \underline{k}) \cong k

$$

when $k$ is an algebraically closed field of characteristic zero. This non-vanishing arises because the etale topology is much finer than the Zariski topology, detecting arithmetic and topological information invisible to the Zariski topology.

**2. Cech cohomology and coverings.** Cech cohomology also depends on the topology. Consider the standard covering $\mathbb{P}^1 = U_0 \cup U_1$ where $U_i \cong \mathbb{A}^1$. When computing Cech cohomology:

- The cover $\{U_0, U_1\}$ is not acyclic (i.e., not a Leray cover) for the constant sheaf $\underline{k}$ in general, neither in the Zariski nor the etale topology. To relate Cech cohomology to sheaf cohomology, one can use the Cech-to-derived-functor spectral sequence

$$
\check{H}^p(\mathfrak{U}, \mathscr{H}^q(\mathscr{F})) \Rightarrow H^{p+q}(X, \mathscr{F})

$$

where $\mathscr{H}^q(\mathscr{F})$ denotes the presheaf $U \mapsto H^q(U, \mathscr{F}|_U)$.

- In the Zariski topology, Cech cohomology with respect to any open affine cover agrees with sheaf cohomology for quasi-coherent sheaves on separated schemes (Leray's theorem). For the constant sheaf $\underline{k}$ (which is not quasi-coherent), this need not hold.

- In the etale topology, Cech cohomology agrees with sheaf cohomology for torsion sheaves on Noetherian schemes under mild hypotheses, but the relevant covers are etale coverings, not just Zariski open covers.

The discrepancy $H^2_{\operatorname{Zar}}(\mathbb{P}^1, \underline{k}) = 0$ versus $H^2_{\text{et}}(\mathbb{P}^1, \underline{k}) \cong k$ highlights that constant sheaves carry very different information in different topologies: the Zariski topology is too coarse to detect the "topological" cohomology that the etale topology captures (and which agrees with singular cohomology for varieties over $\mathbb{C}$ via the comparison theorem).
