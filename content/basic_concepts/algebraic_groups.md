## Algebraic groups and group schemes

### Example: Zariski closure of an algebraic group, not even a group {#ecag-0001}

**Statement:** The Zariski closure of an algebraic group inside an ambient variety need not be an algebraic group. Specifically, the nodal cubic curve $C : y^2 = x^2(x+1)$ over a field $k$ contains an open subvariety isomorphic to $\mathbb{G}_m$, yet $C$ itself is not an algebraic group because it fails to be smooth. This contrasts with the classical result that the Zariski closure of an abstract subgroup $H$ of an algebraic group $G$ is again an algebraic group.

**Construction/Proof:**

*Step 1: The nodal cubic and its smooth locus.* Consider the curve

$$
C : y^2 = x^2(x+1) \subset \mathbb{A}^2_k.

$$

The origin $(0,0)$ is a node: the partial derivatives $\partial_x(x^2(x+1) - y^2) = 3x^2 + 2x$ and $\partial_y(x^2(x+1) - y^2) = -2y$ both vanish at $(0,0)$, while the curve is singular there since the tangent cone $y^2 = x^2$ consists of two distinct lines.

*Step 2: The smooth locus is $\mathbb{G}_m$.* Away from the node, we can parametrize $C \setminus \{(0,0)\}$ by setting $t = y/x$. Then $t^2 = x + 1$, so $x = t^2 - 1$ and $y = t(t^2 - 1)$. This gives an isomorphism

$$
C \setminus \{(0,0)\} \cong \operatorname{Spec}(k[t, t^{-1}]) \cong \mathbb{G}_m.

$$

More precisely, the coordinate ring of $C \setminus \{(0,0)\}$ is generated by $t$ and $t^{-1}$, and the group law on $\mathbb{G}_m$ corresponds to multiplication of the parameter $t$.

*Step 3: The closure is not a group.* The Zariski closure of $C \setminus \{(0,0)\}$ in $\mathbb{A}^2_k$ is the full curve $C$. Since $C$ has a singular point at the origin, it is not smooth, and therefore cannot carry the structure of an algebraic group (every algebraic group over a field is smooth).

*Step 4: Why this does not contradict the closure theorem.* The theorem states: if $G$ is an algebraic group and $H \leq G$ is an abstract subgroup, then $\overline{H}$ is again a subgroup of $G$. In our example, $\mathbb{G}_m$ is not embedded as a subgroup of some ambient algebraic group --- it is embedded as an open subset of the singular variety $C$. The closure is taken inside $C$ (or $\mathbb{A}^2$), not inside an algebraic group.

*Application: $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$ is Zariski-dense in $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$.* By the closure theorem, $\overline{\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})}$ is a closed subgroup of $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$. We show it is the full group using the Bruhat decomposition.

- **Unipotent subgroups:** The matrices $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & n \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ lie in $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$. Their Zariski closure in $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ is $U_+ = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & a \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} : a \in \mathbb{C} \right\}$, since the integer points of $\mathbb{A}^1$ are Zariski-dense. By the closure theorem, $U_+ \subset \overline{\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})}$. Similarly, $U_- = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ a & 1 \end{pmatrix} \right\} \subset \overline{\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})}$.
- **Maximal torus:** Let $T = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} t & 0 \\ 0 & t^{-1} \end{pmatrix} \right\} \cong \mathbb{G}_m$ and $s = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \in \operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$. A direct computation shows

$$
\begin{pmatrix} 1 & x \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ -x^{-1} & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} x & 0 \\ 0 & -x^{-1} \end{pmatrix}.

$$

Since the left-hand side lies in $\overline{\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})}$ (it is a product of elements from $U_+$, $U_-$, and $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$), and since $\overline{\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})}$ is a group, we conclude $T \subset \overline{\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})}$ (after accounting for signs via the element $-I \in \operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$).

- **Bruhat decomposition and conclusion:** The Borel subgroup $B = T U_+ \subset \overline{\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})}$ by the closure theorem. The Bruhat decomposition gives

$$
\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{C}) = U_- B \sqcup U_- s B.

$$

Since $U_-$, $B$, and $s$ all lie in $\overline{\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})}$, and $\overline{\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})}$ is a closed subgroup, we obtain $\overline{\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})} = \operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$.

**Key Insight:** The closure theorem for algebraic groups requires the ambient space to be a group; a group embedded as an open subvariety of a singular variety can have a non-group closure. The application to $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$ illustrates the power of the closure theorem: one only needs to produce enough generators inside the closure and then invoke the group property to bootstrap to the full group.

**Prerequisites:** Algebraic groups, smoothness criterion for group schemes, Zariski topology, Bruhat decomposition for $\operatorname{SL}_2$, multiplicative group $\mathbb{G}_m$

### Example: $SL(2,\mathbb{Z}$ is Zariski-dense in $SL(2,\mathbb{C})$, another proof, Sam {#ecag-0002}

**Statement:** There is an alternative proof that $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$ is Zariski-dense in $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$, using $p$-adic density and the density of $\mathbb{Q}(i)$-points rather than the Bruhat decomposition.

**Construction/Proof:**

Fix a prime $p = 5$ and an embedding $\mathbb{Q}_5 \hookrightarrow \mathbb{C}$ (such embeddings exist by choosing a transcendence basis for $\mathbb{C}$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ and extending). The proof proceeds in five steps.

*Step 1: $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$ is dense in $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z}_5)$ in the $5$-adic topology.* By strong approximation for $\operatorname{SL}_2$ (which is simply connected as an algebraic group), $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$ is dense in $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z}_p)$ for every prime $p$. In particular, for $p = 5$, the closure of $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$ in the $5$-adic topology on $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z}_5)$ is the full group. Since the Zariski topology is coarser than the $5$-adic topology, density in the $5$-adic topology implies Zariski density.

*Step 2: $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z}_5)$ is Zariski-dense in $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Q}_5)$.* The group $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z}_5)$ is an open subset of $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Q}_5)$ in the $5$-adic topology (it is the preimage of $\operatorname{M}_2(\mathbb{Z}_5)$ under the inclusion). An open dense subset in the $p$-adic topology is Zariski-dense, since any polynomial vanishing on an open set of a $p$-adic manifold vanishes identically.

*Step 3: The Zariski closure of $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$ contains $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Q}(i))$.* The field $\mathbb{Q}_5$ contains a primitive $4$th root of unity because $5 \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$, so $x^4 - 1$ splits completely over $\mathbb{Q}_5$. In particular, $i \in \mathbb{Q}_5$, and thus $\mathbb{Q}(i) \subset \mathbb{Q}_5$. Since $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$ is Zariski-dense in $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Q}_5)$ (by Steps 1--2), and $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Q}(i)) \subset \operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Q}_5)$, the Zariski closure of $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$ in $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{C})$ contains $\operatorname{SL}(2,\mathbb{Q}(i))$.

*Step 4: $\operatorname{SL}_2$ contains a dense open isomorphic to $(\mathbb{A}^1 \setminus \{0\})^3$.* The map

$$
(x, y, z) \mapsto \begin{pmatrix} x & y \\ \frac{xz - 1}{y} & z \end{pmatrix}

$$

defines an open embedding $(\mathbb{A}^1 \setminus \{0\})^3 \hookrightarrow \operatorname{SL}_2$, since the determinant is $xz - y \cdot \frac{xz-1}{y} = 1$ and this parametrizes matrices with nonzero entries in the $(1,1)$, $(1,2)$, and $(2,2)$ positions. This is a Zariski-dense open subset of $\operatorname{SL}_2$.

*Step 5: $\mathbb{Q}(i)$-points are dense in $\mathbb{C}$-points.* The set $\mathbb{Q}(i) \setminus \{0\}$ is dense in $\mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}$ in the Euclidean topology (since $\mathbb{Q}(i)$ is dense in $\mathbb{C}$). Therefore $(\mathbb{Q}(i) \setminus \{0\})^3$ is dense in $(\mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\})^3$ in the Euclidean topology, and hence in the Zariski topology. Via the open embedding of Step 4, $\operatorname{SL}(2, \mathbb{Q}(i))$ contains a Zariski-dense subset of $\operatorname{SL}(2, \mathbb{C})$. Since $\operatorname{SL}(2, \mathbb{Q}(i)) \subset \overline{\operatorname{SL}(2, \mathbb{Z})}$ by Step 3, we conclude $\overline{\operatorname{SL}(2, \mathbb{Z})} = \operatorname{SL}(2, \mathbb{C})$.

**Key Insight:** This proof leverages the interplay between $p$-adic and Zariski topologies. The key arithmetic input is strong approximation for $\operatorname{SL}_2$, and the key geometric input is that $\operatorname{SL}_2$ has a large open chart isomorphic to a product of punctured affine lines, over which density of number-field points is easy to establish.

**Prerequisites:** Strong approximation for simply connected groups, $p$-adic topology, Zariski density, Hensel's lemma (for roots of unity in $\mathbb{Q}_5$), density of $\mathbb{Q}(i)$ in $\mathbb{C}$

### Remark: Non-reduced group schemes in characteristic $p$ {#ecag-0003}

In characteristic $p > 0$, the scheme $\operatorname{Spec}(k[x]/(x^p))$ underlies two fundamentally different group scheme structures, arising from the kernels of the Frobenius endomorphism on $\mathbb{G}_m$ and $\mathbb{G}_a$ respectively.

**The two group schemes.** Consider the exact sequences of group schemes over a field $k$ of characteristic $p$:

$$
0 \to \mu_p \to \mathbb{G}_m \xrightarrow{x \mapsto x^p} \mathbb{G}_m \to 0

$$

$$
0 \to \alpha_p \to \mathbb{G}_a \xrightarrow{x \mapsto x^p} \mathbb{G}_a \to 0

$$

Here $\mu_p$ is the kernel of the $p$-th power map on $\mathbb{G}_m$, and $\alpha_p$ is the kernel of the Frobenius on $\mathbb{G}_a$.

**As functors.** The functor $\mu_p$ sends a $k$-algebra $S$ to $\mu_p(S) = \{ s \in S^\times : s^p = 1 \}$, the set of $p$-th roots of unity in $S$. It is represented by $\operatorname{Spec}(k[x]/(x^p - 1))$. The functor $\alpha_p$ sends $S$ to $\alpha_p(S) = \{ s \in S : s^p = 0 \}$, the set of elements whose $p$-th power vanishes. It is represented by $\operatorname{Spec}(k[x]/(x^p))$.

**The subtlety in characteristic $p$.** In characteristic $p$, we have $x^p - 1 = (x - 1)^p$ in $k[x]$, so

$$
k[x]/(x^p - 1) \cong k[x]/((x-1)^p) \cong k[u]/(u^p)

$$

via the substitution $u = x - 1$. Thus $\mu_p$ and $\alpha_p$ have isomorphic underlying schemes, yet they are **non-isomorphic** as group schemes. The distinction lies entirely in the Hopf algebra (comultiplication) structure:

- **For $\mu_p$:** The coordinate ring is $A = k[x]/(x^p - 1)$. The comultiplication is $\Delta(x) = x \otimes x$ (reflecting the multiplicative group law), the counit is $\varepsilon(x) = 1$, and the antipode is $S(x) = x^{p-1} = x^{-1}$.
- **For $\alpha_p$:** The coordinate ring is $A = k[x]/(x^p)$. The comultiplication is $\Delta(x) = x \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x$ (reflecting the additive group law), the counit is $\varepsilon(x) = 0$, and the antipode is $S(x) = -x$.

This example illustrates a fundamental feature of algebraic geometry in positive characteristic: the underlying topological space (or even the underlying scheme) does not determine the group structure. One must work with the full Hopf algebra structure on the coordinate ring.

### Example: A group scheme that is not smooth over a nonperfect field of characteristic $p$ {#ecag-0004}

**Statement:** Over a nonperfect field $k$ of characteristic $p > 0$, the closed subgroup scheme $V(x^p + \alpha y^p) \subset \mathbb{G}_{a,k}^2$ (where $\alpha \in k$ is not a $p$-th power) is reduced and irreducible but fails to be smooth and even fails to be normal.

**Construction/Proof:**

*Step 1: Setup.* Let $k$ be a field of characteristic $p > 0$ and let $\alpha \in k \setminus k^p$. Consider the additive group $\mathbb{G}_{a,k}^2 = \operatorname{Spec}(k[x,y])$ with group law given by componentwise addition. Define

$$
G = V(x^p + \alpha y^p) \subset \mathbb{G}_{a,k}^2.

$$

*Step 2: $G$ is a subgroup scheme.* We verify that the ideal $(x^p + \alpha y^p)$ defines a closed subgroup scheme. The comultiplication on $\mathbb{G}_{a,k}^2$ sends $(x, y) \mapsto (x \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x, \, y \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes y)$. Then

$$
(x \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x)^p + \alpha (y \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes y)^p = (x^p + \alpha y^p) \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes (x^p + \alpha y^p)

$$

since all cross-terms vanish in characteristic $p$ (the binomial coefficients $\binom{p}{i}$ for $0 < i < p$ are divisible by $p$). This lies in the ideal generated by $x^p + \alpha y^p$ in the tensor product, confirming $G$ is a subgroup scheme. Similarly, the counit sends $x, y \mapsto 0$, and the antipode sends $(x,y) \mapsto (-x, -y)$, both preserving the defining ideal.

*Step 3: $G$ is reduced and irreducible.* The coordinate ring is $k[x,y]/(x^p + \alpha y^p)$. Over the algebraic closure $\overline{k}$, we have $x^p + \alpha y^p = (x + \alpha^{1/p} y)^p$, but since $\alpha^{1/p} \notin k$, the polynomial $f(x,y) = x^p + \alpha y^p$ is irreducible over $k$. Indeed, if $f = gh$ with $g, h \in k[x,y]$ of positive degree, then over $\overline{k}$ we would have $(x + \alpha^{1/p} y)^p = g \cdot h$, forcing $g = (x + \alpha^{1/p} y)^a$ and $h = (x + \alpha^{1/p} y)^b$ with $a + b = p$ over $\overline{k}$. Expanding and comparing coefficients forces $\alpha^{a/p} \in k$ (from the coefficient of $y^a$ in $g$), which for $0 < a < p$ implies $\alpha^{1/p} \in k$ (since $\gcd(a,p) = 1$), a contradiction.

Since $f$ is irreducible, $(f)$ is a prime ideal, so $G$ is integral (reduced and irreducible).

*Step 4: $G$ is not smooth.* The Jacobian criterion gives smoothness: the partial derivatives are

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = p x^{p-1} = 0, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = \alpha p y^{p-1} = 0

$$

since $\operatorname{char}(k) = p$. The Jacobian matrix vanishes identically on $G$, so every point of $G$ is singular. In particular, $G$ is nowhere smooth over $k$.

*Step 5: $G$ is not normal.* Over $\overline{k}$, the base change $G_{\overline{k}} = V((x + \alpha^{1/p} y)^p) \subset \mathbb{G}_{a,\overline{k}}^2$ is a non-reduced scheme (a "fat line" with multiplicity $p$). The coordinate ring of $G_{\overline{k}}$ is $\overline{k}[x,y]/((x + \alpha^{1/p}y)^p)$, which has nilpotents. Since normality is preserved under base change for varieties over a field, and $G_{\overline{k}}$ is non-reduced (hence not normal), $G$ itself is not normal. Alternatively, one can directly verify that the fraction field of $k[x,y]/(x^p + \alpha y^p)$ has elements integral over the coordinate ring that do not lie in it: the element $x/y$ satisfies $(x/y)^p = -\alpha \in k$, so $x/y$ is integral over $k[y]/(x^p + \alpha y^p)$, but $x/y$ does not lie in the coordinate ring.

**Key Insight:** In characteristic $p$ over nonperfect fields, the Frobenius morphism creates pathological phenomena: a polynomial can be irreducible (hence define a reduced variety) while simultaneously having vanishing partial derivatives everywhere (hence being nowhere smooth). The example shows that "reduced + irreducible" does not imply "smooth" or even "normal" for group schemes in positive characteristic, in stark contrast with characteristic zero where every algebraic group is smooth.

**Prerequisites:** Group schemes, Frobenius endomorphism, Jacobian criterion for smoothness, irreducibility over nonperfect fields, normality of varieties
