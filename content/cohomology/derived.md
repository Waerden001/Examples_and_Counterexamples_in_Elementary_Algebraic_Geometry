## Derived categories

### Example: Dualizing complex of a projective variety {#ecag-0215}

**Statement:** Let $X = \operatorname{Proj}(k[x,y,z,w]/(xy, xz)) \subset \mathbf{P}^3$ be the union of a plane $V(x)$ and a line $V(y,z)$. The dualizing complex of $X$ is

$$
\omega_X^{\bullet} = \mathbf{R}\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{P}^3}}(\mathcal{O}_X, \omega_{\mathbf{P}^3}[3]).

$$

We compute $\omega_X^{\bullet}$ explicitly as a complex of line bundles on $\mathbf{P}^3$ restricted to $X$.

**Construction/Proof:** The ideal $(xy, xz) = (x) \cap (y,z)$ is a monomial ideal, so a free resolution of $\mathcal{O}_X$ over $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{P}^3}$ can be read off from the generators and their syzygies. The minimal free resolution is

$$
\mathcal{L}^{\bullet}: 0 \rightarrow \mathcal{O}(-3) \xrightarrow{\begin{bmatrix} z \\ -y \end{bmatrix}} \mathcal{O}(-2) \oplus \mathcal{O}(-2) \xrightarrow{\begin{bmatrix} xy & xz \end{bmatrix}} \mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{P}^3} \rightarrow \mathcal{O}_X \rightarrow 0.

$$

To verify exactness: the map $\mathcal{O}(-3) \to \mathcal{O}(-2)^{\oplus 2}$ sends $1 \mapsto (z, -y)$, and composing gives $xy \cdot z + xz \cdot (-y) = 0$, confirming this is a complex. The kernel of $\begin{bmatrix} xy & xz \end{bmatrix}$ consists of pairs $(a,b)$ with $x(ay + bz) = 0$; since $x$ is not a zero divisor in $k[x,y,z,w]$, we need $ay + bz = 0$, whose syzygies are generated by $(z, -y)$.

Since $\omega_{\mathbf{P}^3} = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{P}^3}(-4)$, we have

$$
\mathbf{R}\operatorname{Hom}(\mathcal{O}_X, \omega_{\mathbf{P}^3}[3]) \cong \operatorname{Hom}(\mathcal{L}^{\bullet}, \mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{P}^3}(-4)[3]).

$$

The second isomorphism holds because $\mathcal{L}^{\bullet} \to \mathcal{O}_X$ is a quasi-isomorphism and each $\mathcal{L}^i$ is locally free, so $\operatorname{Hom}(\mathcal{L}^{\bullet}, -)$ computes $\mathbf{R}\operatorname{Hom}(\mathcal{O}_X, -)$.

Dualizing the complex (applying $\operatorname{Hom}(-, \mathcal{O}(-4))$) and shifting by $[3]$ reverses the arrows, transposes the matrices, and twists by $\mathcal{O}(-4)$:

$$
\omega_X^{\bullet}: \left[\mathcal{O}(-4) \xrightarrow{\begin{bmatrix} xy \\ xz \end{bmatrix}} \mathcal{O}(-2) \oplus \mathcal{O}(-2) \xrightarrow{\begin{bmatrix} z & -y \end{bmatrix}} \mathcal{O}(-1)\right][-3].

$$

Here the term $\mathcal{O}(-4)$ sits in cohomological degree $0$ after the shift, and the complex is placed in degrees $0, 1, 2$ before the $[-3]$ shift (i.e., in degrees $-3, -2, -1$ after shifting).

Note that $X$ is not Cohen-Macaulay: the dualizing complex is a genuine complex (not a single sheaf in one degree). The plane component $V(x) \cong \mathbf{P}^2$ is Cohen-Macaulay of dimension 2, and the line component $V(y,z) \cong \mathbf{P}^1$ is Cohen-Macaulay of dimension 1, but their union has components of different dimensions, so $X$ is not equidimensional and hence not Cohen-Macaulay.

**Key Insight:** For monomial ideals in polynomial rings, minimal free resolutions are combinatorially explicit (via Taylor or Scarf complexes), making dualizing complex computations completely concrete. The failure of $\omega_X^{\bullet}$ to be a single sheaf directly detects the non-Cohen-Macaulay nature of $X$.

**Prerequisites:** Dualizing complex, Grothendieck duality, locally free resolutions, Cohen-Macaulay condition, canonical sheaf of projective space

### Example: Complexes have the same homology but not quasi-isomorphic {#ecag-0216}

**Statement:** The following two complexes of $\mathbf{C}[x,y]$-modules have isomorphic homology in every degree, yet are not quasi-isomorphic (i.e., not isomorphic in the derived category $D(\operatorname{Mod}_{\mathbf{C}[x,y]})$):

$$
\mathcal{L}^{\bullet}: \mathbf{C}[x,y] \oplus \mathbf{C}[x,y] \xrightarrow{\begin{bmatrix} x & y \end{bmatrix}} \mathbf{C}[x,y],

$$

$$
\mathcal{M}^{\bullet}: \mathbf{C}[x,y] \xrightarrow{0} \mathbf{C}.

$$

In both complexes, the rightmost term is placed in cohomological degree $0$.

**Construction/Proof:** First, we compute the homology of both complexes.

For $\mathcal{L}^{\bullet}$: The map $(a,b) \mapsto ax + by$ is surjective onto the ideal $(x,y)$, so $H^0(\mathcal{L}^{\bullet}) = \mathbf{C}[x,y]/(x,y) \cong \mathbf{C}$. The kernel of $\begin{bmatrix} x & y \end{bmatrix}$ consists of syzygies $(a,b)$ with $ax + by = 0$. The first syzygy module of the ideal $(x,y)$ in $\mathbf{C}[x,y]$ is generated by $(y, -x)$, so $H^{-1}(\mathcal{L}^{\bullet}) \cong \mathbf{C}[x,y] \cdot (y,-x) \cong \mathbf{C}[x,y]$.

For $\mathcal{M}^{\bullet}$: The map is zero, so $H^0(\mathcal{M}^{\bullet}) = \mathbf{C}$ and $H^{-1}(\mathcal{M}^{\bullet}) = \mathbf{C}[x,y]$.

Thus both complexes have $H^{-1} \cong \mathbf{C}[x,y]$ and $H^0 \cong \mathbf{C}$, with all other homology vanishing.

Now we show they are not quasi-isomorphic. Suppose $\phi: \mathcal{M}^{\bullet} \to \mathcal{L}^{\bullet}$ is a morphism of complexes. Then we have a commutative diagram: $\phi^{-1}: \mathbf{C}[x,y] \to \mathbf{C}[x,y]^{\oplus 2}$ and $\phi^{0}: \mathbf{C} \to \mathbf{C}[x,y]$. The map $\phi^0$ sends $1 \mapsto c$ for some constant $c \in \mathbf{C}$. If $\phi$ is a quasi-isomorphism, then $\phi^0$ induces an isomorphism on $H^0$, so $c \neq 0$. But the commutativity condition requires the image of $\phi^{-1}$ to land in $\ker\begin{bmatrix} x & y \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{C}[x,y] \cdot (y,-x)$, since the differential in $\mathcal{M}^{\bullet}$ is zero and then $\begin{bmatrix} x & y \end{bmatrix} \circ \phi^{-1} = \phi^0 \circ 0 = 0$. So $\phi^{-1}(1) = f(x,y)(y,-x)$ for some polynomial $f$. For $\phi$ to induce an isomorphism on $H^{-1}$, the map $\phi^{-1}$ must send a generator of $\mathbf{C}[x,y]$ to a generator of $\mathbf{C}[x,y] \cdot (y,-x)$, which means $f$ must be a unit, so $f \in \mathbf{C}^{\times}$. This all works, but we must also check that no roof $\mathcal{L}^{\bullet} \xleftarrow{\sim} \mathcal{K}^{\bullet} \to \mathcal{M}^{\bullet}$ gives an isomorphism.

A more decisive argument uses $\operatorname{Ext}$: if $\mathcal{L}^{\bullet}$ and $\mathcal{M}^{\bullet}$ were quasi-isomorphic, then $\operatorname{Hom}_{D}(\mathcal{L}^{\bullet}, \mathcal{L}^{\bullet}) \cong \operatorname{Hom}_{D}(\mathcal{M}^{\bullet}, \mathcal{M}^{\bullet})$. But $\operatorname{Hom}_{D}(\mathcal{M}^{\bullet}, \mathcal{M}^{\bullet}) \cong \operatorname{Hom}_{D}(\mathbf{C}[x,y][-1], \mathbf{C}[x,y][-1]) \oplus \operatorname{Hom}_{D}(\mathbf{C}, \mathbf{C}) \oplus \operatorname{Hom}_{D}(\mathbf{C}[x,y][-1], \mathbf{C}) \oplus \operatorname{Hom}_{D}(\mathbf{C}, \mathbf{C}[x,y][-1])$, since $\mathcal{M}^{\bullet}$ splits as a direct sum in the derived category (its differential is zero). In contrast, $\mathcal{L}^{\bullet}$ does not split because $\operatorname{Ext}^1_{\mathbf{C}[x,y]}(\mathbf{C}, \mathbf{C}[x,y]) \neq 0$ — indeed, applying $\operatorname{Hom}(\mathbf{C}, -)$ to the Koszul resolution shows this Ext group is nonzero. Thus $\mathcal{L}^{\bullet}$ represents a nontrivial extension in the derived category that $\mathcal{M}^{\bullet}$ does not.

**Key Insight:** The derived category remembers more than just homology — it also records extension data between homology objects. Two complexes with the same homology can differ by a nontrivial extension class in $\operatorname{Ext}^1(H^0, H^{-1}[1])$, preventing them from being quasi-isomorphic.

**Prerequisites:** Derived categories, quasi-isomorphisms, Koszul complex, Ext groups, syzygies

## Derived pushforward and shifted direct sum of higher direct images
Consider the simplest springer resolution
$$f:X=T^{*}\mathbf{P}^{1}\rightarrow \operatorname{Spec\,}(k[x,y,z]/xy-z^{2})=Y$$. Since $f$ is a semismall map, $\mathbf{R} f_{*}(\mathbf{Q}_{X}[2])$ is a perverse sheaf on $Y$.
We claim

$$\mathbf{R} f_{*}(\mathbf{Q}_{X}[2])\neq \mathbf{R}^{0}f_{*}(\mathbf{Q}_{X}[2])\oplus \mathbf{R}^{-1}f_{*}(\mathbf{Q}_{X}[2])[1]\oplus \mathbf{R}^{-2}f_{*}(\mathbf{Q}_{X}[2])[2].$$

### Remark {#ecag-0217}

Let $f: X \rightarrow Y$ be a proper morphism between irreducible varieties. By semi-continuity of fibre dimension, the locus

$$
Y_k := \{y \in Y \mid \dim f^{-1}(y) \geq k\}

$$

is a closed subset of $Y$ for each $k \geq 0$. The morphism $f$ is called **semismall** if it satisfies the dimension estimate

$$
2k + \dim Y_k \leq \dim X

$$

for all $k \geq 0$, or equivalently, $k + \dim Y_k \leq \dim X - k$. The map $f$ is called **small** if strict inequality holds for all $k \geq 1$. These conditions constrain the trade-off between the size of the locus $Y_k$ and the dimension of the fibres over that locus: larger fibres can only occur over smaller-dimensional strata. For the Springer resolution $f: T^*\mathbf{P}^1 \to \operatorname{Spec}(k[x,y,z]/(xy - z^2))$, we have $\dim X = 2$, the generic fibre is a point ($k = 0$), and the fibre over the vertex is $\mathbf{P}^1$ ($k = 1$). Then $Y_1 = \{0\}$ has dimension 0, so $2 \cdot 1 + 0 = 2 = \dim X$, confirming semismallness. When $f$ is semismall, $\mathbf{R}f_*(\mathbf{Q}_X[\dim X])$ is a perverse sheaf, which is a much stronger structural constraint than merely being an object of the derived category.

## Fourier-Mukai transforms on elliptic curves
Let $E$ be an elliptic curve over $\mathbf{C}$. $\mathscr{P}$ be the normalized Poincare bundle on $E\times E$ defined as follows
<!-- tikzcd diagram: manual conversion needed -->
```latex
\begin{tikzcd}
 & \mathscr{P}=\pi_{1}^{*}\mathcal{O}_{E}(-p_{0})\otimes \pi_{2}^{*}\mathcal{O}_{E}(-p_{0})\otimes \mathcal{O}_{E\times E}(\Delta)\arrow{d}&\\
 &E\times E\arrow{dl}{\pi_{1}}\arrow{dr}{\pi_{2}} &\\
  E& &E
\end{tikzcd}
```.$$
Then we have

$$\mathscr{P}|_{p\times E}=\mathcal{O}_{E}(p-p_{0}), \mathscr{P}|_{E\times p}=\mathcal{O}_{E}(p-p_{0}).$$

We consider the Fourier-Mukai transform

$$\Phi:=\Phi^{\mathscr{P}}_{E\rightarrow E}: \mathrm{D}^{b}(E)\rightarrow \mathrm{D}^{b}(E)$$

$$\mathscr{E}\mapsto \mathbf{R}\pi_{2, *}(\pi_{1}^{*}\mathscr{E}\otimes\mathscr{P} ).$$

Mukai shows us the following theorem.

### Theorem: Mukai {#ecag-0218}

**Statement:** Let $E$ be an elliptic curve over $\mathbf{C}$ with origin $p_0$, and let $\mathscr{P} = \pi_1^*\mathcal{O}_E(-p_0) \otimes \pi_2^*\mathcal{O}_E(-p_0) \otimes \mathcal{O}_{E \times E}(\Delta)$ be the normalized Poincare bundle on $E \times E$. Define the Fourier-Mukai transform $\Phi: \mathrm{D}^b(E) \to \mathrm{D}^b(E)$ by $\Phi(\mathscr{E}) = \mathbf{R}\pi_{2,*}(\pi_1^*\mathscr{E} \otimes \mathscr{P})$. Then

$$
\Phi \circ \Phi = \iota \circ [-1],

$$

where $\iota: E \to E$ is the negation map $p \mapsto -p$ in the group law and $[-1]$ denotes the shift functor. In particular, $\Phi$ is an autoequivalence of $\mathrm{D}^b(E)$.

**Construction/Proof:** The Fourier-Mukai kernel of the composition $\Phi \circ \Phi$ is the convolution

$$
\mathscr{P} \circ \mathscr{P} := \mathbf{R}\pi_{13,*}(\pi_{12}^*\mathscr{P} \otimes \pi_{23}^*\mathscr{P}),

$$

where $\pi_{ij}$ are projections from $E \times E \times E$. The strategy is to simplify $\pi_{12}^*\mathscr{P} \otimes \pi_{23}^*\mathscr{P}$ using the see-saw principle (Lemma ecag-0220 below), then apply flat base change.

**Step 1.** By Lemma ecag-0220, we have the isomorphism of line bundles on $E \times E \times E$:

$$
\pi_{12}^*\mathscr{P} \otimes \pi_{23}^*\mathscr{P} \cong f^*\mathscr{P},

$$

where $f: E \times E \times E \to E \times E$ is defined by $f(x,y,z) = (x+z, y)$.

**Step 2.** Consider the commutative diagram

$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
E \times E \times E & \xrightarrow{f} & E \times E \\
\downarrow^{\pi_{13}} & & \downarrow^{\pi_1} \\
E \times E & \xrightarrow{m} & E
\end{array}

$$

where $m(x,z) = x + z$ is the addition map. The square is cartesian: given $(x,z) \in E \times E$ and $e \in E$, the fibre product consists of $(x, y, z)$ with $y = e$, $x + z = m(x,z)$. Since $\pi_1: E \times E \to E$ is flat (it is a projection), flat base change gives

$$
\mathbf{R}\pi_{13,*}(f^*\mathscr{P}) = m^*(\mathbf{R}\pi_{1,*}\mathscr{P}).

$$

**Step 3.** We compute $\mathbf{R}\pi_{1,*}\mathscr{P}$. For any closed point $p \in E$, the restriction $\mathscr{P}|_{p \times E} = \mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0)$. When $p \neq p_0$, this is a degree-zero line bundle that is nontrivial, so $H^0(E, \mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0)) = 0$ and $H^1(E, \mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0)) = 0$ (by Riemann-Roch on a curve of genus 1, $\chi = 0$, and $H^0 = 0$ for a nontrivial degree-0 bundle implies $H^1 = 0$). When $p = p_0$, $\mathscr{P}|_{p_0 \times E} = \mathcal{O}_E$, so $H^0 = k$ and $H^1 = k$.

Since $H^1(\mathscr{P}|_{p \times E}) = 0$ for $p \neq p_0$ (an open dense set) and $\mathscr{P}$ is a line bundle (hence flat over $E$ via $\pi_1$), cohomology and base change applies. We get:

- $R^0\pi_{1,*}\mathscr{P} = 0$: the sheafification of $U \mapsto H^0(\pi_1^{-1}(U), \mathscr{P})$ vanishes because $H^0(\mathscr{P}|_{p \times E}) = 0$ for general $p$.
- $R^1\pi_{1,*}\mathscr{P} = \mathcal{O}_{p_0}$: the skyscraper sheaf at $p_0$, since $H^1$ jumps only at $p_0$.

Therefore $\mathbf{R}\pi_{1,*}\mathscr{P} = \mathcal{O}_{p_0}[-1]$.

**Step 4.** Pulling back via $m$:

$$
m^*(\mathcal{O}_{p_0}[-1]) = \mathcal{O}_{\Gamma_\iota}[-1],

$$

where $\Gamma_\iota = \{(x, z) \in E \times E \mid x + z = p_0\} = \{(x, -x)\}$ is the graph of the negation map $\iota$. Thus

$$
\mathscr{P} \circ \mathscr{P} = \mathcal{O}_{\Gamma_\iota}[-1].

$$

The Fourier-Mukai transform with kernel $\mathcal{O}_{\Gamma_\iota}[-1]$ is precisely $\iota^* \circ [-1] = \iota \circ [-1]$, since $\mathcal{O}_{\Gamma_\iota}$ is the kernel of the pullback $\iota^*$. This proves $\Phi \circ \Phi = \iota \circ [-1]$.

Since $\iota^2 = \operatorname{id}$ and $[-1]$ commutes with $\iota$, we have $(\Phi \circ \Phi)^2 = \iota^2 \circ [-2] = [-2]$, so $\Phi^4 = [-2]$. In particular $\Phi$ is an equivalence with quasi-inverse $\Phi^{-1} = \iota \circ [1] \circ \Phi$.

**Key Insight:** The computation reduces to evaluating $\mathbf{R}\pi_{1,*}\mathscr{P}$, which collapses to a skyscraper sheaf $\mathcal{O}_{p_0}[-1]$ because the Poincare bundle restricted to fibres is generically a nontrivial degree-zero line bundle (hence acyclic). Pulling back this skyscraper along the addition map recovers the graph of the negation, giving the elegant formula $\Phi^2 = \iota \circ [-1]$.

**Prerequisites:** Fourier-Mukai transforms, Poincare bundle, cohomology and base change, flat base change, derived categories, elliptic curves

The fact that $\mathbf{R} \pi_{13, *}(\pi_{12}^{*}\mathscr{P}\otimes \pi_{23}^{*}\mathscr{P})=\mathbf{R} \pi_{13, *}(f^{*}\mathscr{P})$ comes from the following two lemmas. First for any closed point $p\in E$, we denote the fibre product  $m^{-1}(p)$ by

$$D_{p}:=\{(x, y)\in E\times E|x+y=p\}.$$

### Lemma {#ecag-0219}

**Statement:** For any closed point $p \in E$, the following isomorphism of line bundles on $E \times E$ holds:

$$
\mathcal{O}_{E \times E}(D_p - D_{p_0}) \cong \pi_1^*\mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0) \otimes \pi_2^*\mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0),

$$

where $D_p = \{(x,y) \in E \times E \mid x + y = p\}$ is the fibre of the addition map $m: E \times E \to E$ over $p$.

**Construction/Proof:** We apply the see-saw principle: two line bundles on a product $A \times B$ of complete varieties are isomorphic if and only if their restrictions to $\{a\} \times B$ agree for all closed points $a$ and their restrictions to $A \times \{b_0\}$ agree for some closed point $b_0$.

Restricting to $\{q\} \times E$ for any closed point $q \in E$:

$$
\pi_1^*\mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0) \otimes \pi_2^*\mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0)\big|_{q \times E} = \mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0).

$$

For the left-hand side, $D_p \cap (\{q\} \times E) = \{(q, p - q)\}$ and $D_{p_0} \cap (\{q\} \times E) = \{(q, p_0 - q)\}$, so

$$
\mathcal{O}_{E \times E}(D_p - D_{p_0})\big|_{q \times E} = \mathcal{O}_E((p-q) - (p_0-q)) = \mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0).

$$

The restrictions agree on all fibres $\{q\} \times E$.

Restricting to $E \times \{q\}$:

$$
\pi_1^*\mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0) \otimes \pi_2^*\mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0)\big|_{E \times q} = \mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0).

$$

Similarly, $\mathcal{O}_{E \times E}(D_p - D_{p_0})\big|_{E \times q} = \mathcal{O}_E((p-q) - (p_0-q)) = \mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0)$.

By the see-saw principle, the two line bundles are isomorphic.

**Key Insight:** The see-saw principle reduces verifying an isomorphism of line bundles on a product to checking agreement on fibres, turning a global geometric statement into pointwise computations on the elliptic curve.

**Prerequisites:** See-saw principle, line bundles on abelian varieties, elliptic curve group law

### Lemma {#ecag-0220}

**Statement:** Let $f: E \times E \times E \to E \times E$ be defined by $f(x,y,z) = (x+z, y)$. There is an isomorphism of line bundles on $E \times E \times E$:

$$
\pi_{12}^*\mathscr{P} \otimes \pi_{23}^*\mathscr{P} \cong f^*\mathscr{P},

$$

where $\mathscr{P} = \pi_1^*\mathcal{O}_E(-p_0) \otimes \pi_2^*\mathcal{O}_E(-p_0) \otimes \mathcal{O}_{E \times E}(\Delta)$ is the normalized Poincare bundle on $E \times E$.

**Construction/Proof:** We again use the see-saw principle, now on $E \times E \times E$ viewed as a family over the second factor $E$.

**Restriction to $E \times \{p\} \times E$:**

For $\pi_{12}^*\mathscr{P}$: this restricts to $\mathscr{P}|_{E \times p} = \mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0)$ pulled back to $E \times E$ via the first projection. That is, $\pi_{12}^*\mathscr{P}|_{E \times p \times E} = \pi_1^*\mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0)$.

For $\pi_{23}^*\mathscr{P}$: this restricts to $\mathscr{P}|_{p \times E} = \mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0)$ pulled back via the third coordinate. That is, $\pi_{23}^*\mathscr{P}|_{E \times p \times E} = \pi_2^*\mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0)$ (where $\pi_1, \pi_2$ now denote the two projections from the slice $E \times E$).

So

$$
(\pi_{12}^*\mathscr{P} \otimes \pi_{23}^*\mathscr{P})|_{E \times p \times E} = \pi_1^*\mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0) \otimes \pi_2^*\mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0).

$$

For $f^*\mathscr{P}$: the map $f$ restricted to $E \times \{p\} \times E$ sends $(x,z) \mapsto (x+z, p)$. We have

$$
f^*\mathscr{P}|_{E \times p \times E} = f^*(\pi_1^*\mathcal{O}_E(-p_0) \otimes \pi_2^*\mathcal{O}_E(-p_0) \otimes \mathcal{O}(\Delta))|_{E \times p \times E}.

$$

Since $f^*\pi_2^*\mathcal{O}_E(-p_0)|_{E \times p \times E} = \mathcal{O}_E(-p_0)$ (constant, pulled back from the $p$-factor, hence trivial on $E \times E$ up to a constant twist). More precisely:

- $f^*\mathcal{O}(\Delta)|_{E \times p \times E} = \mathcal{O}_{E \times E}(D_p)$, since $\Delta = \{(a,b) \mid a = b\}$ pulls back under $(x,z) \mapsto (x+z, p)$ to $\{x + z = p\} = D_p$.
- $f^*\pi_1^*\mathcal{O}_E(-p_0)|_{E \times p \times E} = \mathcal{O}_{E \times E}(-D_{p_0})$, via the same reasoning (the composition $\pi_1 \circ f: (x,z) \mapsto x+z$ restricted gives the addition map $m$).
- $f^*\pi_2^*\mathcal{O}_E(-p_0)|_{E \times p \times E} = 0$ (this is the trivial bundle, as $\pi_2 \circ f = \pi_2$ restricted to the slice gives the constant map to $p$, so the pullback is $\mathcal{O}_E(p_0 - p_0) = \mathcal{O}$... more carefully, $f^*\pi_2^* = \pi_{\text{middle}}^*$ and restricting to $E \times \{p\} \times E$ gives a trivial contribution).

Thus $f^*\mathscr{P}|_{E \times p \times E} = \mathcal{O}_{E \times E}(D_p - D_{p_0})$, which by Lemma ecag-0219 equals $\pi_1^*\mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0) \otimes \pi_2^*\mathcal{O}_E(p - p_0)$.

**Restriction to $\{p_0\} \times E \times \{p_0\}$:**

All three bundles $\pi_{12}^*\mathscr{P}$, $\pi_{23}^*\mathscr{P}$, and $f^*\mathscr{P}$ restrict to the trivial bundle on $\{p_0\} \times E \times \{p_0\}$. For $\pi_{12}^*\mathscr{P}$: it restricts to $\mathscr{P}|_{p_0 \times E} = \mathcal{O}_E(p_0 - p_0) = \mathcal{O}_E$. For $\pi_{23}^*\mathscr{P}$: it restricts to $\mathscr{P}|_{E \times p_0} = \mathcal{O}_E(p_0 - p_0) = \mathcal{O}_E$. For $f^*\mathscr{P}$: $f(p_0, y, p_0) = (p_0, y)$, so it restricts to $\mathscr{P}|_{p_0 \times E} = \mathcal{O}_E$.

By the see-saw principle applied to the family over the second factor $E$, we conclude

$$
\pi_{12}^*\mathscr{P} \otimes \pi_{23}^*\mathscr{P} \cong f^*\mathscr{P}.

$$

**Key Insight:** The key identity reduces the convolution of the Poincare bundle with itself to a simple pullback, which then becomes amenable to flat base change. This works because the see-saw principle, combined with Lemma ecag-0219, identifies the fibrewise restrictions of both sides.

**Prerequisites:** See-saw principle, Poincare bundle, line bundles on products of abelian varieties, Lemma ecag-0219

We can actually prove that $\Phi$ is an equivalence quite easily by proving $\Phi(\mathcal{O}_{x})=\mathscr{P}_{x}$ maps an orthonormal basis to another orthonormal basis and commutes with the Serre functor, see for example [Andrei Căldăraru's note, p25](http://www.math.wisc.edu/~andreic/publications/lnPoland.pdf). Everything boils down to the following two simple facts

$$\mathrm{Ext}^{i}(\mathcal{O}_{E}(p'-p_{0}), \mathcal{O}_{E}(p-p_{0}))=\mathrm{H}^{i}(\mathcal{O}_{E}(p-p'))=0 \text{ if $p\neq p'$ or $i\neq 0, 1$}$$

$$\mathscr{P}_{x}\otimes \omega_{E}=\mathscr{P}_{x}.$$

## Six functors

### Example: Adjoint functor {#ecag-0221}

**Statement:** Let $i: (\mathbf{Z}, \leq) \hookrightarrow (\mathbf{R}, \leq)$ be the inclusion of posets (viewed as categories where there is a unique morphism $a \to b$ if and only if $a \leq b$). The inclusion $i$ has a right adjoint given by the floor function $\lfloor \cdot \rfloor: (\mathbf{R}, \leq) \to (\mathbf{Z}, \leq)$, and a left adjoint given by the ceiling function $\lceil \cdot \rceil: (\mathbf{R}, \leq) \to (\mathbf{Z}, \leq)$.

**Construction/Proof:** We verify the adjunction $i \dashv \lfloor \cdot \rfloor$. The adjunction means: for all $n \in \mathbf{Z}$ and $r \in \mathbf{R}$,

$$
\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathbf{R}}(i(n), r) \cong \operatorname{Hom}_{\mathbf{Z}}(n, \lfloor r \rfloor).

$$

In a poset category, $\operatorname{Hom}(a,b)$ is a singleton if $a \leq b$ and empty otherwise. So the adjunction says:

$$
i(n) \leq r \iff n \leq \lfloor r \rfloor,

$$

which is precisely the defining property of the floor function: $n \leq \lfloor r \rfloor$ if and only if $n \leq r$ for integers $n$.

Similarly, $\lceil \cdot \rceil \dashv i$ is the left adjoint: $\lceil r \rceil \leq n$ if and only if $r \leq i(n) = n$, which is the defining property of the ceiling function.

This example illustrates the philosophy that adjoint functors are "best approximations": $\lfloor r \rfloor$ is the best integer approximation to $r$ from below (the largest integer $\leq r$), while $\lceil r \rceil$ is the best approximation from above (the smallest integer $\geq r$). In the six-functor formalism, adjunctions like $(f^*, f_*)$, $(f_!, f^!)$ encode analogous "best approximation" relationships between categories of sheaves on different spaces.

**Key Insight:** Adjunctions between poset categories reduce to Galois connections, making the "best approximation" interpretation of adjoint functors completely transparent. The floor function is the right adjoint to inclusion because it is the best integer approximation from below.

**Prerequisites:** Categories, adjoint functors, posets as categories, Galois connections

### Example: $i_{*},i^{*}, i_{!}, i^{!}, j_{*},j^{*}, j_{!}, j^{!}$ {#ecag-0222}

**Statement:** Consider the stratification of $\mathbf{C}$ (with the analytic topology) into the open stratum $\mathbf{C}^{\times}$ and the closed point $\{0\}$, with inclusion maps

$$
\mathbf{C}^{\times} \xhookrightarrow{i} \mathbf{C} \xhookleftarrow{j} \{0\}.

$$

We describe the six-functor behavior for the constant sheaf on each stratum and explain the key distinctions between proper and non-proper embeddings.

**Construction/Proof:**

*Properness.* The closed embedding $j: \{0\} \hookrightarrow \mathbf{C}$ is proper (a closed immersion of finite type is always proper), so $j_* = j_!$. The open embedding $i: \mathbf{C}^{\times} \hookrightarrow \mathbf{C}$ is not proper: the punctured line $\mathbf{C}^{\times} \to \mathbf{C}$ fails the valuative criterion because the generic point of $\operatorname{Spec}(\mathbf{C}[[t]])$ maps to $\mathbf{C}^{\times}$ (via $t \mapsto t$) but the closed point maps to $0 \in \mathbf{C} \setminus \mathbf{C}^{\times}$. Geometrically, the image of the map $t \mapsto t$ in $\mathbf{C}^{\times}$ has a limit point $0$ that lies outside $\mathbf{C}^{\times}$, violating universal closedness. For an open embedding, $i_* \neq i_!$ in general: $i_*$ is the full direct image (extension by nearby sections), while $i_!$ is extension by zero (compact support).

*Vanishing under "wrong" compositions.* A fundamental property of the six-functor formalism on a complementary open-closed pair $(i, j)$:

- $j^*i_! = 0$ and $j^!i_* = 0$: extending a sheaf from the open stratum (by zero or by sections) and then restricting to the closed stratum yields zero.
- $i^*j_! = 0$ and $i^!j_* = 0$: extending a sheaf from the closed stratum and restricting to the open stratum yields zero.

These follow from the exact triangles $j_!j^! \to \operatorname{id} \to i_*i^*$ and $i_!i^! \to \operatorname{id} \to j_*j^*$.

*Equality $i^* = i^!$.* For an open embedding $i$, we have $i^* = i^! = i^{-1}$ (restriction to the open subset). This is because $i$ is etale, so $i^!$ differs from $i^*$ only by a twist by the relative dualizing sheaf, which is trivial for an etale morphism.

*Intuitive understanding of properness.* The valuation criterion says that a morphism $f: X \to Y$ is proper if for every DVR $R$ with fraction field $K$, every $K$-point of $X$ lying over a $\operatorname{Spec}(R)$-point of $Y$ extends uniquely to an $R$-point of $X$. The spectrum of a DVR serves as an algebraic substitute for a small curve: $\operatorname{Spec}(K)$ is the generic point (the "curve minus a point") and $\operatorname{Spec}(R)$ is the whole curve. Properness thus says that "curves in the image are closed" — any arc approaching the boundary must have its limit in the space.

**Key Insight:** The complementary open-closed pair $(i, j)$ with $i$ open and $j$ closed gives rise to exact triangles that force "first extend then restrict by different functors" to vanish. The distinction $j_* = j_!$ (closed, hence proper) versus $i_* \neq i_!$ (open, not proper) is the fundamental asymmetry in the six-functor formalism.

**Prerequisites:** Six-functor formalism, proper and open immersions, direct and inverse image functors, extension by zero, valuative criterion of properness, DVR

### Remark {#ecag-0223}

The cohomology and base change theorem (smooth or proper base change) can fail for sheaves of $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules when one expects it to hold for constructible sheaves. Consider the stratification $\mathbf{C}^{\times} \xhookrightarrow{i} \mathbf{C} \xhookleftarrow{j} \{0\}$. For the $\mathcal{O}$-module direct image, we have

$$
j^*i_*\mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{C}^{\times}} = 0.

$$

This is an algebraic statement: the stalk of $i_*\mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{C}^{\times}}$ at $0$ involves the ring of sections of $\mathcal{O}$ on punctured neighborhoods of $0$. In the algebraic category, $i_*\mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{C}^{\times}}$ corresponds to the $k[t]$-module $k[t, t^{-1}]$, and pulling back to $\{0\}$ via $j$ gives

$$
k[t]/t \otimes_{k[t]} k[t, t^{-1}] = 0,

$$

since every element of $k[t,t^{-1}]$ is killed by a power of $t$ modulo $k[t]$: explicitly, $t^{-n}$ maps to $t^{-n} \otimes 1 = 1 \otimes t^{-n} \cdot t^n \cdot t^{-n}$... more directly, the tensor product is the localization $(k[t]/(t))_{t} = 0$ since $t$ is both invertible and zero. This vanishing is in contrast to the behavior of the constant sheaf $\mathbf{Q}_{\mathbf{C}^{\times}}$, where $j^*i_*\mathbf{Q}_{\mathbf{C}^{\times}} \neq 0$ due to the topological phenomenon of monodromy and nearby cycles.

### Remark: Why the difference? {#ecag-0224}

The discrepancy between the behavior of $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules and local systems (constructible sheaves) under operations like $j^*i_*$ reflects a fundamental difference in what these categories "see." Local systems (or more generally, constructible sheaves) are governed by the topology of the space: they encode monodromy around singular points, nearby cycles, and the fundamental group. The stalk $(i_*\mathscr{L})_0$ for a local system $\mathscr{L}$ on $\mathbf{C}^{\times}$ involves sections on a small punctured disk, which can be nontrivial due to the nontrivial topology of $\mathbf{C}^{\times}$ (its fundamental group $\pi_1(\mathbf{C}^{\times}) \cong \mathbf{Z}$).

In contrast, $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules incorporate the algebraic structure sheaf, and the pullback $j^*i_*\mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{C}^{\times}}$ involves an algebraic tensor product that kills the Laurent polynomial information. The structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}$ imposes algebraic regularity conditions that are much more rigid than topological conditions. In the algebraic setting, $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{C}^{\times}} = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{C}}[t^{-1}]$ is obtained by inverting $t$, and tensoring with $\mathcal{O}_{\{0\}} = k$ over $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{C}} = k[t]$ annihilates everything because $t$ acts as both a unit (in $k[t,t^{-1}]$) and as zero (in $k[t]/(t)$).

The deeper reason is that $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules detect the scheme-theoretic structure of the closed point (the maximal ideal and its powers), while local systems only detect the topological complement. This is why D-modules and the Riemann-Hilbert correspondence are needed to bridge the two worlds: a D-module on $\mathbf{C}$ encodes both the algebraic differential equation structure and the topological monodromy data of its solutions.
