## Picard groups

### Example: Why do we need the separateness condition? Picard group of an affine line with double origins {#ecag-0250}

**Statement:** Let $k$ be a field and let $X = \overline{\mathbb{A}}_{k} \setminus \{0\}$ be the affine line with double origins over $k$. Then:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(X) \cong \mathbb{Z}, \quad \operatorname{CaCl}(X) \cong \mathbb{Z}, \quad \operatorname{Cl}(X) \cong \mathbb{Z}.

$$

Moreover, the canonical maps $\operatorname{CaCl}(X) \to \operatorname{Cl}(X)$ and $\operatorname{CaCl}(X) \to \operatorname{Pic}(X)$ are isomorphisms, so the separatedness hypothesis is not needed for these identifications in this case.

**Construction/Proof:** Recall the definitions:

- $\operatorname{Pic}(X) := H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^*)$,
- $\operatorname{CaCl}(X) := H^0(X, \mathcal{M}^* / \mathcal{O}_X^*) / H^0(X, \mathcal{M}^*)$,
- $\operatorname{Cl}(X) := \operatorname{Div}(X) / \operatorname{div}(k(X))$.

The scheme $X$ is constructed by gluing two copies $U_1 = \operatorname{Spec}(k[t])$ and $U_2 = \operatorname{Spec}(k[t])$ along $U_1 \setminus \{0\} \cong U_2 \setminus \{0\} = \operatorname{Spec}(k[t, t^{-1}])$ via the identity map.

**Step 1.** $X$ is Noetherian, integral, and locally factorial (each local ring is a DVR or a field), hence normal and regular in codimension 1.

**Step 2.** Since $X$ is factorial, every Weil divisor is locally principal, so $\operatorname{CaCl}(X) \cong \operatorname{Cl}(X)$.

**Step 3.** Since $X$ is integral, the natural map $\operatorname{CaCl}(X) \to \operatorname{Pic}(X)$ is an isomorphism.

**Step 4.** To compute $\operatorname{Pic}(X)$, use the Cech cohomology with respect to the open cover $\{U_1, U_2\}$. The Cech complex for $\mathcal{O}_X^*$ gives:

$$
H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^*) \cong \mathcal{O}_X^*(U_1 \cap U_2) / (\mathcal{O}_X^*(U_1) \cdot \mathcal{O}_X^*(U_2)) = k[t, t^{-1}]^* / (k^* \cdot k^*).

$$

Since $k[t, t^{-1}]^* = k^* \times \mathbb{Z}$ (generated by units and powers of $t$), the quotient is $\mathbb{Z}$.

**Key Insight:** The affine line with double origins is non-separated but still Noetherian, integral, and locally factorial, so all three notions of divisor class group agree and can be computed via Cech cohomology on the standard cover.

**Prerequisites:** Cech cohomology, line bundles, Picard group, Cartier and Weil divisors, non-separated schemes

### Remark: Relations between $\operatorname{Cl}, \operatorname{CaCl}, \operatorname{Pic}$ {#ecag-0251}

The precise relationships among the three divisor class groups are as follows.

**Hypotheses for definitions.** To define all three groups, one typically requires $X$ to be Noetherian (for finiteness), integral (to have a well-defined function field), and regular in codimension 1 (e.g., normal, so that codimension-1 local rings are DVRs). Separatedness is traditionally assumed but is not strictly necessary for $\operatorname{CaCl}$ or $\operatorname{Pic}$.

**The map $\operatorname{CaCl}(X) \to \operatorname{Cl}(X)$.** This map sends a Cartier divisor to its associated Weil divisor. It is injective in general. It is an isomorphism when $X$ is factorial (every local ring is a UFD), because in a UFD every height-1 prime ideal is principal, which means every Weil divisor is locally principal, hence Cartier.

**The map $\operatorname{CaCl}(X) \to \operatorname{Pic}(X)$.** This map sends a Cartier divisor class to its associated line bundle $\mathcal{O}_X(D)$. It is injective even without the Noetherian assumption. For surjectivity, one needs every line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ to embed into the sheaf $\mathcal{M}$ of rational functions. The key requirement is that the restriction map

$$
i : \mathcal{O}_X(U) \to \mathcal{O}_X(U \cap U')

$$

is injective for suitable opens $U, U'$. This holds when $X$ is reduced (nilradicals vanish, so sections are determined by their values at generic points). In practice, $X$ integral suffices.

### Example: Another way to compute the Picard group of an affine line with double origins {#ecag-0252}

**Statement:** Let $X = \overline{\mathbb{A}}_k \setminus \{0\}$ be the affine line with double origins over a field $k$. Then $\operatorname{Pic}(X) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, and a generator is the line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ that is trivial on each copy of $\mathbb{A}^1_k$ and whose transition function on $\mathbb{A}^1_k \setminus \{0\}$ is multiplication by $t$.

**Construction/Proof:** We use the Mayer--Vietoris sequence for the sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X^*$ on the open cover $\{U_1, U_2\}$, where $U_1 \cong U_2 \cong \mathbb{A}^1_k$.

**Step 1.** Since $U_1$ and $U_2$ are affine and $\operatorname{Pic}(\mathbb{A}^1_k) = 0$ (the polynomial ring $k[t]$ is a PID), we have $H^1(U_i, \mathcal{O}_{U_i}^*) = 0$ for $i = 1, 2$.

**Step 2.** The Mayer--Vietoris exact sequence gives:

$$
\mathcal{O}_X^*(U_1) \times \mathcal{O}_X^*(U_2) \xrightarrow{\delta} \mathcal{O}_X^*(U_1 \cap U_2) \to H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^*) \to 0.

$$

**Step 3.** We have $\mathcal{O}_X^*(U_i) = k^*$ and $\mathcal{O}_X^*(U_1 \cap U_2) = k[t, t^{-1}]^* = k^* \times t^{\mathbb{Z}}$. The map $\delta$ sends $(a, b) \mapsto ab^{-1}$, so its image is $k^*$. Therefore:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(X) \cong k[t, t^{-1}]^* / k^* \cong \mathbb{Z}.

$$

**Step 4.** The generator corresponds to the transition function $t$ on the overlap. Explicitly, the line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ is $\mathcal{O}_{U_1}$ on $U_1$ and $\mathcal{O}_{U_2}$ on $U_2$, glued by $s \mapsto t \cdot s$ on $U_1 \cap U_2$. This bundle is nontrivial because $t$ is not a unit in either $k[t]$.

**Key Insight:** The Mayer--Vietoris sequence reduces the computation to the unit groups of the pieces and their overlap, bypassing explicit divisor computations.

**Prerequisites:** Mayer--Vietoris sequence for sheaf cohomology, unit groups of polynomial and Laurent polynomial rings, non-separated schemes

### Remark {#ecag-0253}

The groups $\operatorname{CaCl}(X)$ and $\operatorname{Pic}(X)$ can be defined for any scheme $X$ without any Noetherian hypothesis: $\operatorname{Pic}(X) = H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^*)$ is a sheaf cohomology group, and $\operatorname{CaCl}(X) = H^0(X, \mathcal{M}^*/\mathcal{O}_X^*) / H^0(X, \mathcal{M}^*)$ uses only the sheaves of total quotient rings. In contrast, the Weil divisor class group $\operatorname{Cl}(X)$ requires $X$ to be Noetherian (or at least locally Noetherian), integral, and regular in codimension 1, since its definition involves codimension-1 points and their associated DVRs. This is why $\operatorname{Cl}(X)$ is the most restrictive of the three notions.

### Example: Hartshorne, Example $\mathrm{II}.6.11.4$, $\operatorname{CaCl}^{0}$ {#ecag-0254}

**Statement:** Let $C$ be a nonsingular projective curve of genus $g$ over an algebraically closed field $k$. The degree-zero part of the Cartier class group $\operatorname{CaCl}^0(C)$ is the group of divisor classes of degree zero. The natural map $\operatorname{CaCl}^0(C) \to \operatorname{Pic}^0(C)$ is an isomorphism, and $\operatorname{Pic}^0(C)$ is the group of $k$-rational points of the Jacobian variety $J(C)$, an abelian variety of dimension $g$.

**Construction/Proof:**

**Step 1.** Since $C$ is nonsingular, it is locally factorial, so $\operatorname{CaCl}(C) \cong \operatorname{Cl}(C) \cong \operatorname{Pic}(C)$. The degree map $\deg : \operatorname{Cl}(C) \to \mathbb{Z}$ is a surjective homomorphism, and its kernel is $\operatorname{Cl}^0(C) = \operatorname{CaCl}^0(C) = \operatorname{Pic}^0(C)$.

**Step 2.** When $g = 0$, i.e., $C \cong \mathbb{P}^1_k$, we have $\operatorname{Pic}(\mathbb{P}^1) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ generated by $\mathcal{O}(1)$, so $\operatorname{Pic}^0(\mathbb{P}^1) = 0$. The Jacobian is trivial.

**Step 3.** When $g = 1$, i.e., $C$ is an elliptic curve, the Abel--Jacobi map provides an isomorphism $\operatorname{Pic}^0(C) \cong C(k)$ as groups (where $C$ acquires its group law from a chosen base point). This is the content of Hartshorne II.6.10.2 and II.6.11.4.

**Step 4.** For general $g \geq 1$, the Jacobian variety $J(C)$ is an abelian variety of dimension $g$, and the Abel--Jacobi theorem gives $\operatorname{Pic}^0(C) \cong J(C)(k)$. The short exact sequence is:

$$
0 \to \operatorname{Pic}^0(C) \to \operatorname{Pic}(C) \xrightarrow{\deg} \mathbb{Z} \to 0.

$$

**Key Insight:** For a smooth projective curve, the kernel of the degree map on $\operatorname{Pic}$ is a rich geometric object (the Jacobian), whose dimension equals the genus and which carries the structure of an abelian variety.

**Prerequisites:** Divisors on curves, degree map, Abel--Jacobi theorem, Jacobian variety, genus

### Example: Hartshorne, $\mathrm{II}.6.9$, Picard groups of singular curves {#ecag-0255}

**Statement:** Let $C$ be a reduced irreducible curve over an algebraically closed field $k$, and let $f : \widetilde{C} \to C$ be its normalization. There is an exact sequence:

$$
0 \to \bigoplus_{p \in C} \widetilde{\mathcal{O}}_{C,p}^* / \mathcal{O}_{C,p}^* \to \operatorname{Pic}(C) \to \operatorname{Pic}(\widetilde{C}) \to 0

$$

where the direct sum is over the singular points $p$ of $C$, and $\widetilde{\mathcal{O}}_{C,p}$ denotes the integral closure of $\mathcal{O}_{C,p}$.

**Construction/Proof:**

**Step 1.** The normalization $f : \widetilde{C} \to C$ is a finite birational morphism. There is a short exact sequence of sheaves on $C$:

$$
0 \to \mathcal{O}_C^* \to f_* \mathcal{O}_{\widetilde{C}}^* \to f_* \mathcal{O}_{\widetilde{C}}^* / \mathcal{O}_C^* \to 0.

$$

**Step 2.** The quotient sheaf $f_* \mathcal{O}_{\widetilde{C}}^* / \mathcal{O}_C^*$ is supported only at the singular points of $C$, since $f$ is an isomorphism over the smooth locus.

**Step 3.** Taking the long exact sequence in cohomology and using $H^1(C, f_* \mathcal{O}_{\widetilde{C}}^*) \cong H^1(\widetilde{C}, \mathcal{O}_{\widetilde{C}}^*) = \operatorname{Pic}(\widetilde{C})$ (by the Leray spectral sequence, since $f$ is affine), we obtain the stated exact sequence.

**Step 4 (Example: cuspidal cubic).** Let $C : y^2 = x^3$ in $\mathbb{A}^2_k$. The normalization is $\widetilde{C} \cong \mathbb{A}^1_k$ via $t \mapsto (t^2, t^3)$. The unique singular point is the origin $p = (0,0)$. We have $\mathcal{O}_{C,p} = k[t^2, t^3]_{(t^2, t^3)}$ and $\widetilde{\mathcal{O}}_{C,p} = k[t]_{(t)}$. The quotient $\widetilde{\mathcal{O}}_{C,p}^* / \mathcal{O}_{C,p}^*$ is identified with the additive group $\mathbb{G}_a$, since the units that differ are of the form $1 + ct$ for $c \in k$. Since $\operatorname{Pic}(\mathbb{A}^1_k) = 0$, we get $\operatorname{Pic}(C) \cong \mathbb{G}_a = (k, +)$.

**Step 5 (Example: nodal cubic).** Let $C : y^2 = x^2(x+1)$ in $\mathbb{A}^2_k$. The normalization is again $\widetilde{C} \cong \mathbb{A}^1_k$. At the node $p = (0,0)$, the integral closure has two branches, and $\widetilde{\mathcal{O}}_{C,p}^* / \mathcal{O}_{C,p}^* \cong \mathbb{G}_m = k^*$. Hence $\operatorname{Pic}(C) \cong \mathbb{G}_m = (k^*, \cdot)$.

**Key Insight:** The Picard group of a singular curve is controlled by a local-to-global exact sequence in which the local terms at singular points measure the discrepancy between the curve and its normalization, and these local contributions can be either additive (cusps) or multiplicative (nodes).

**Prerequisites:** Normalization of curves, conductor ideal, Leray spectral sequence, local rings at singular points, cuspidal and nodal singularities

### Example: Hartshorne $\mathrm{II}.7.13$, a complete nonprojective surface, ??? still some problems {#ecag-0256}

**Statement:** Let $k$ be an algebraically closed field with $\operatorname{char}(k) \neq 2$. Let $C : y^2 z = x^3 \subset \mathbb{P}^2_k$ be the cuspidal cubic. There exists a complete (proper over $k$) surface $X$ fibered over $\mathbb{P}^1_k$ with fibers isomorphic to $C$, such that $\operatorname{Pic}(X) \cong \mathbb{G}_a$. Consequently, $X$ is not projective.

**Construction/Proof:** The surface $X$ is constructed as a twisted family of cuspidal cubics over $\mathbb{P}^1_k$. Explicitly, take two copies of $C \times_k \mathbb{A}^1_k$ and identify them along $C \times (\mathbb{A}^1_k \setminus \{0\})$ via the morphism $(x, t) \sim (\phi_t(x), t^{-1})$, where $\phi_t$ is an appropriate automorphism of $C$ parameterized by $t$. This gives a surface $X$ with a natural morphism:

$$
\pi : X \to \mathbb{P}^1_k.

$$

**Step 1 (Completeness).** Properness is local on the base. Over $D_+(x_0)$ and $D_+(x_1)$ of $\mathbb{P}^1_k$, the morphism $\pi$ is isomorphic to $C \times \mathbb{A}^1_k \to \mathbb{A}^1_k$, which is a base change of the proper morphism $C \to \operatorname{Spec}(k)$. Hence $\pi$ is proper, and since $\mathbb{P}^1_k$ is proper over $k$, the composition $X \to \operatorname{Spec}(k)$ is proper.

**Step 2 (Picard group of $C \times \mathbb{A}^1_k$).** The normalization of $C \times \mathbb{A}^1_k$ is $f : \mathbb{P}^1_k \times \mathbb{A}^1_k \to C \times \mathbb{A}^1_k$. By the method of Hartshorne II.6.9, we get the exact sequence:

$$
0 \to \bigoplus_{p \in C \times \mathbb{A}^1_k} \widetilde{\mathcal{O}}_p / \mathcal{O}_p \to \operatorname{Pic}(C \times \mathbb{A}^1_k) \to \operatorname{Pic}(\mathbb{P}^1_k \times \mathbb{A}^1_k) \to 0.

$$

The singular locus of $C \times \mathbb{A}^1_k$ is the codimension-1 subscheme $\{0\} \times \mathbb{A}^1_k$, where:

$$
\widetilde{\mathcal{O}}_p = k[t, z]_{(t)}, \quad \mathcal{O}_p = k[t^2, t^3, z]_{(t^2, t^3)}.

$$

The quotient detects only the degree-1 monomials $ct$ for $c \in k$, giving $\widetilde{\mathcal{O}}_p^* / \mathcal{O}_p^* \cong \mathbb{G}_a$. Since $\operatorname{Pic}(\mathbb{P}^1_k \times \mathbb{A}^1_k) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, the sequence becomes:

$$
0 \to \mathbb{G}_a \to \operatorname{Pic}(C \times \mathbb{A}^1_k) \to \mathbb{Z} \to 0.

$$

A section (sending $1 \in \mathbb{Z}$ to the pullback of $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(1)$) splits this sequence:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(C \times \mathbb{A}^1_k) \cong \mathbb{G}_a \times \mathbb{Z}.

$$

**Step 3 (Picard group of $C \times (\mathbb{A}^1_k \setminus \{0\})$).** By the same method, using the fact that $A[z, z^{-1}]^* \cong A^* \times \mathbb{Z}$ for any integral domain $A$, we get:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(C \times (\mathbb{A}^1_k \setminus \{0\})) \cong \mathbb{G}_a \times \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}.

$$

**Step 4 (Restriction map).** The restriction $r : \operatorname{Pic}(C \times \mathbb{A}^1_k) \to \operatorname{Pic}(C \times (\mathbb{A}^1_k \setminus \{0\}))$ can be analyzed via the commutative diagram of normalization exact sequences. The map $i : \mathbb{G}_a \to \mathbb{G}_a \times \mathbb{Z}$ is the injection into the first component, and the map on normalizations $j : \operatorname{Pic}(\mathbb{P}^1_k \times \mathbb{A}^1_k) \to \operatorname{Pic}(\mathbb{P}^1_k \times (\mathbb{A}^1_k \setminus \{0\}))$ is an isomorphism on the $\mathbb{Z}$-factor. Thus the restriction is:

$$
r : \mathbb{G}_a \times \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{G}_a \times \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}, \quad (c, n) \mapsto (c, 0, n).

$$

**Step 5 (Mayer--Vietoris for $X$).** Writing $X = V_1 \cup V_2$ where $V_i \cong C \times \mathbb{A}^1_k$ and $V_1 \cap V_2 \cong C \times (\mathbb{A}^1_k \setminus \{0\})$, the Mayer--Vietoris sequence for $\mathcal{O}_X^*$ gives:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(V_1) \times \operatorname{Pic}(V_2) \xrightarrow{r_1 - r_2} \operatorname{Pic}(V_1 \cap V_2) \to H^2(X, \mathcal{O}_X^*).

$$

Computing the cokernel of the difference of restriction maps yields $\operatorname{Pic}(X) \cong \mathbb{G}_a$.

**Key Insight:** The twisted gluing of cuspidal cubics over $\mathbb{P}^1$ produces a complete surface whose Picard group is the additive group $\mathbb{G}_a$, which is not finitely generated. Since any projective variety has a finitely generated Picard group (by the Neron--Severi theorem), this forces $X$ to be nonprojective.

**Prerequisites:** Normalization exact sequence, Picard groups of products, Mayer--Vietoris for sheaf cohomology, properness, cuspidal cubics

### Remark: Why does $\operatorname{Pic}(X) \cong \mathbb{G}_{a}$ implies $X$ is not projective? {#ecag-0257}

If $X$ were projective over $k$, then by the Neron--Severi theorem, the Picard group $\operatorname{Pic}(X)$ would fit into an exact sequence:

$$
0 \to \operatorname{Pic}^0(X) \to \operatorname{Pic}(X) \to \operatorname{NS}(X) \to 0

$$

where $\operatorname{NS}(X)$ is a finitely generated abelian group (the Neron--Severi group). Moreover, for a projective variety, $\operatorname{Pic}^0(X)$ is an abelian variety (the Picard variety), in particular a projective algebraic group. However, $\mathbb{G}_a$ is not an abelian variety: it is a unipotent group of dimension 1, affine rather than projective, and it has no nontrivial algebraic quotient that is an abelian variety. More concretely, for a projective variety $X$ with an ample line bundle $\mathcal{L}$, the powers $\mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}$ for $n \geq 1$ give a free abelian subgroup $\mathbb{Z} \hookrightarrow \operatorname{Pic}(X)$, but $\mathbb{G}_a \cong (k, +)$ has no element of infinite order when $\operatorname{char}(k) > 0$, and even in characteristic zero, $\mathbb{G}_a$ cannot contain a copy of $\mathbb{Z}$ as a Zariski-closed algebraic subgroup generating an ample system. Therefore $X$ cannot be projective.

### Remark {#ecag-0258}

Since most schemes in the Hartshorne II.7.13 example are not regular in codimension 1 (the cuspidal cubic $C$ is singular at the cusp), the excision exact sequence for Weil divisors does not apply. Specifically, we do **not** have:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(Y) \to \operatorname{Pic}(X) \to \operatorname{Pic}(X \setminus Y) \to 0.

$$

This sequence arises from the exact sequence for Weil divisor class groups $\operatorname{Cl}$, which requires $X$ to be normal and regular in codimension 1. In our situation, we cannot even define $\operatorname{Cl}(X)$ for $C \times \mathbb{A}^1_k$ in the usual sense because $C$ has a non-normal singular point. Instead, one must work directly with the Picard group (line bundles / Cartier divisors) and the normalization exact sequence.

### Example: Hironaka varieties, smooth complete nonprojective $3$-folds {#ecag-0259}

**Statement:** There exists a smooth complete (proper over $\mathbb{C}$) threefold $X$ that is not projective. Hironaka's construction produces such examples by performing birational modifications (small resolutions with different choices) on a smooth projective threefold along two intersecting curves.

**Construction/Proof:**

**Step 1 (Setup).** Start with a smooth projective threefold $Y$ over $\mathbb{C}$ and two smooth curves $C_1, C_2 \subset Y$ that meet transversally at exactly two points $p$ and $q$.

**Step 2 (Local modification).** In a neighborhood of $p$, first blow up along $C_1$ and then blow up the strict transform of $C_2$. In a neighborhood of $q$, reverse the order: first blow up along $C_2$, then blow up the strict transform of $C_1$. Away from $p$ and $q$, the two curves are disjoint, and one performs ordinary blow-ups along each.

**Step 3 (Resulting threefold).** The resulting threefold $X$ is smooth and complete. The fibers over $p$ and $q$ each contain a copy of $\mathbb{P}^1$, call them $\ell_p$ and $\ell_q$. By the reversed order of blow-ups, the curve $\ell_p$ arises as the exceptional fiber of the blow-up of $C_1$ at $p$, while $\ell_q$ arises from the blow-up of $C_1$ at $q$ (or vice versa, depending on convention).

**Step 4 (Nonprojectivity).** The key observation is that the curves $\ell_p$ and $\ell_q$ are numerically equivalent but satisfy $\ell_p + \ell_q \sim 0$ as a 1-cycle (they form the boundary of a 2-chain). This means any ample divisor $H$ would need to satisfy $H \cdot \ell_p > 0$ and $H \cdot \ell_q > 0$, but $H \cdot (\ell_p + \ell_q) = 0$, a contradiction. More precisely, one shows that $\ell_p$ and $\ell_q$ are algebraically equivalent to zero, which prevents the existence of an ample divisor by the Nakai--Moishezon criterion.

**Step 5 (Formal argument).** By the Nakai--Moishezon criterion, a divisor $D$ on a complete variety is ample if and only if $D^{\dim V} \cdot V > 0$ for every subvariety $V$. The existence of complete curves $\ell$ with $D \cdot \ell \leq 0$ for all divisors $D$ obstructs ampleness of any divisor, proving nonprojectivity.

**Key Insight:** The asymmetric blow-up procedure at two points creates a pair of exceptional curves whose numerical properties prevent any divisor from being ample, obstructing projectivity while preserving smoothness and completeness.

**Prerequisites:** Blow-ups, exceptional divisors, Nakai--Moishezon ampleness criterion, algebraic equivalence of cycles, proper varieties

### Example: $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}}(1)$ is not necessarily ample {#ecag-0260}

**Statement:** Let $S$ be a scheme and $\mathscr{E}$ a locally free sheaf on $S$. The tautological line bundle $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}}(1)$ on the projective bundle $\pi : \mathbb{P}\mathscr{E} \to S$ is always relatively ample (ample relative to $\pi$), but it is not necessarily ample in the absolute sense.

**Construction/Proof:**

**Step 1 (Relative ampleness).** By definition, $\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E} = \operatorname{Proj}(\operatorname{Sym}(\mathscr{E}))$, and $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}}(1)$ is the universal quotient line bundle. It is $\pi$-ample by construction (it corresponds to the Serre twist on fibers).

**Step 2 (Counterexample).** Let $S = \mathbb{P}^1_k$ and let $\mathscr{E} = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(1) \oplus \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(-1)$. Then $\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}$ is a ruled surface (Hirzebruch surface $\mathbb{F}_2$). The tautological bundle $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}}(1)$ restricts to $\mathcal{O}(1)$ on each fiber $\mathbb{P}^1$, but it is not ample on $\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}$.

**Step 3 (Why it fails).** On $\mathbb{F}_2$, there is a section $\sigma_0 : \mathbb{P}^1 \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}$ corresponding to the surjection $\mathscr{E} \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(-1)$. This section satisfies $\sigma_0^2 = -2$. For any line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ to be ample, we need $\mathcal{L} \cdot \sigma_0 > 0$. But $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}}(1) \cdot \sigma_0 = \deg(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(-1)) = -1 < 0$. By the Nakai--Moishezon criterion, $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}}(1)$ is not ample.

**Step 4 (When it is ample).** $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}}(1)$ is ample (absolutely) if and only if $\mathscr{E}$ is an ample vector bundle on $S$. For a curve $S$, this means every quotient line bundle of $\mathscr{E}$ has positive degree.

**Key Insight:** Relative ampleness does not imply absolute ampleness; the failure is detected by sections of the projective bundle corresponding to quotients of $\mathscr{E}$ with non-positive degree.

**Prerequisites:** Projective bundles, tautological line bundle, Hirzebruch surfaces, Nakai--Moishezon criterion, ample vector bundles

### Remark: Degree of $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}}(1)$ {#ecag-0261}

For a locally free sheaf $\mathscr{E}$ of rank $r$ on a smooth projective curve $C$ of genus $g$, the projective bundle $\pi : \mathbb{P}\mathscr{E} \to C$ is a smooth projective variety of dimension $r$, and $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}}(1)$ has the following intersection-theoretic properties. On each fiber $\mathbb{P}^{r-1}$, the restriction of $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}}(1)$ is $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^{r-1}}(1)$, so it has degree 1 along fibers. The top self-intersection on $\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}$ is:

$$
c_1(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}\mathscr{E}}(1))^r = \deg(\mathscr{E}).

$$

This is a consequence of the relation $\pi_*(c_1(\mathcal{O}(1))^{r-1}) = 1$ and the defining relation $c_1(\mathcal{O}(1))^r - c_1(\pi^*\mathscr{E}) \cdot c_1(\mathcal{O}(1))^{r-1} + \cdots = 0$ in the Chow ring. In the rank-2 case on a curve, $c_1(\mathcal{O}(1))^2 = \deg(\mathscr{E})$. Comparing with the curve case: for a line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ on $C$, $\mathbb{P}\mathcal{L} = C$ and $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}\mathcal{L}}(1) = \mathcal{L}$, so the "degree" is just $\deg(\mathcal{L})$. Thus the formula $c_1(\mathcal{O}(1))^r = \deg(\mathscr{E})$ generalizes the classical degree of a line bundle on a curve to higher-rank bundles.

### Example: $\operatorname{Cl}(X)$ of quadric hypersurfaces, Hartshorne $\mathrm{II}.6.5$ {#ecag-0262}

**Statement:** Let $k$ be a field with $\operatorname{char}(k) \neq 2$, and let $X$ be the affine quadric hypersurface

$$
X = \operatorname{Spec}(k[x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n] / (x_0^2 + x_1^2 + \cdots + x_r^2)).

$$

Then $X$ is normal for $r \geq 2$ (Hartshorne II.6.4), and:

- If $r = 2$: $\operatorname{Cl}(X) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.
- If $r = 3$: $\operatorname{Cl}(X) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.
- If $r \geq 4$: $\operatorname{Cl}(X) = 0$, and consequently the coordinate ring is a UFD.

For the projective quadric $Q = \operatorname{Proj}(k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]/(x_0^2 + \cdots + x_r^2))$:

- If $r = 2$: $Q \cong \mathbb{P}^1$, so $\operatorname{Cl}(Q) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.
- If $r = 3$: $Q \cong \mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ (Segre embedding), so $\operatorname{Cl}(Q) \cong \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}$.
- If $r \geq 4$: $\operatorname{Cl}(Q) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ (by the Lefschetz hyperplane theorem).

**Construction/Proof:**

*Case $r = 2$:* Consider the example $X = \operatorname{Spec}(k[x, y, z]/(xy - z^2))$. Let $Y$ be the prime divisor defined by the ideal $(y, z)$. Localizing at the generic point of $Y$, the DVR is:

$$
(k[x, y, z]/(xy - z^2))_{(y, z)}, \quad \mathfrak{m} = (z), \quad (y) = (z^2).

$$

So $\operatorname{div}(y) = 2Y$. The excision exact sequence gives:

$$
\mathbb{Z} \to \operatorname{Cl}(X) \to \operatorname{Cl}(X \setminus Y) \to 0.

$$

Since $X \setminus Y \cong \operatorname{Spec}(k[y, y^{-1}, z])$ is the spectrum of a UFD, $\operatorname{Cl}(X \setminus Y) = 0$. Hence $\operatorname{Cl}(X)$ is generated by $[Y]$ and $2[Y] = 0$. If $\operatorname{Cl}(X) = 0$, then $Y$ would be principal, meaning $(y, z)$ is principal in $k[x, y, z]/(xy - z^2)$. But $\mathfrak{m}_p / \mathfrak{m}_p^2$ at $p = (x, y, z)$ is a 3-dimensional $k$-vector space spanned by $\bar{x}, \bar{y}, \bar{z}$, which shows $(y, z)$ cannot be generated by a single element. Hence $\operatorname{Cl}(X) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

*Case $r = 3$:* The affine quadric $X$ is the cone over $V = \operatorname{Proj}(k[x, y, z, w]/(xy - z^2 - w^2))$. By Hartshorne II.6.3(b):

$$
\mathbb{Z} \to \operatorname{Cl}(V) \to \operatorname{Cl}(X) \to 0

$$

where $1 \mapsto [H \cap V]$ (a hyperplane section). Since $V \cong \mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ via the Segre embedding, $\operatorname{Cl}(V) \cong \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}$. The hyperplane class maps to $(1, 1) \in \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}$, so:

$$
\operatorname{Cl}(X) \cong (\mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}) / \mathbb{Z}(1, 1) \cong \mathbb{Z}.

$$

*Case $r \geq 4$:* By the Grothendieck--Lefschetz hyperplane theorem, for a smooth projective quadric $V$ of dimension $\geq 3$, the restriction $\operatorname{Cl}(\mathbb{P}^n) \to \operatorname{Cl}(V)$ is an isomorphism. Hence $\operatorname{Cl}(V) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ generated by the hyperplane class. The sequence $\mathbb{Z} \to \operatorname{Cl}(V) \to \operatorname{Cl}(X) \to 0$ then gives $\operatorname{Cl}(X) = 0$. By Hartshorne II.6.2, since $X$ is normal with trivial class group, the coordinate ring is a UFD.

**Key Insight:** The class group of affine quadric cones is governed by the relationship between the class group of the corresponding projective quadric and the hyperplane class; the codimension of the embedding determines whether torsion, free, or trivial behavior occurs.

**Prerequisites:** Weil divisor class group, excision exact sequence, affine cones, Segre embedding, Lefschetz hyperplane theorem, UFD criterion

### Remark: Hartshorne $\mathrm{II}.6.3$, relations between $\operatorname{Cl}(V)$ and $\operatorname{Cl}(C(V))$ {#ecag-0263}

Let $V \subset \mathbb{P}^n_k$ be a normal projective variety and $C(V) = \operatorname{Spec}(S(V))$ its affine cone, where $S(V)$ is the homogeneous coordinate ring. Hartshorne II.6.3 establishes the following exact sequence:

$$
\mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\alpha} \operatorname{Cl}(V) \xrightarrow{\beta} \operatorname{Cl}(C(V)) \to 0

$$

where the map $\alpha$ sends $1 \in \mathbb{Z}$ to the class of a hyperplane section $[H \cap V]$ in $\operatorname{Cl}(V)$.

The key points are:

1. **Surjectivity of $\beta$:** Every Weil divisor on $C(V)$ restricts to a Weil divisor on $V$ (after removing the vertex), and conversely every divisor on $V$ extends to one on $C(V) \setminus \{\text{vertex}\}$ by taking the cone. Since $C(V) \setminus \{\text{vertex}\} \to V$ is an $\mathbb{A}^1 \setminus \{0\}$-bundle, we have $\operatorname{Cl}(C(V) \setminus \{\text{vertex}\}) \cong \operatorname{Cl}(V)$, and the excision exact sequence for the vertex gives exactness on the right.

2. **Image of $\alpha$:** Two divisors on $V$ that differ by a hyperplane section become linearly equivalent on $C(V)$, because the hyperplane section on $V$ corresponds to the principal divisor of a degree-1 element in $S(V)$ on the cone.

3. **Applications:** $\operatorname{Cl}(C(V)) = 0$ if and only if $\operatorname{Cl}(V)$ is generated by the hyperplane class. By Hartshorne II.6.2, $\operatorname{Cl}(C(V)) = 0$ implies $S(V)$ is a UFD.

### Remark: Lefschetz hyperplane theorem {#ecag-0264}

The Lefschetz hyperplane theorem (in Grothendieck's algebraic version, SGA 2) has the following consequence for divisor class groups. If $V \subset \mathbb{P}^n_k$ is a smooth complete intersection of dimension $\geq 3$, then the restriction map $\operatorname{Cl}(\mathbb{P}^n) \to \operatorname{Cl}(V)$ is an isomorphism. In particular, $\operatorname{Cl}(V) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, generated by the hyperplane class.

Applied to quadric hypersurfaces $Q \subset \mathbb{P}^n_k$ with $\dim Q \geq 3$ (i.e., $r \geq 4$ in the notation of the preceding example), this gives $\operatorname{Cl}(Q) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, which implies that the affine cone has trivial class group.

As a corollary, we obtain **Klein's theorem**: if $r \geq 4$ and $Y$ is an irreducible codimension-1 subvariety of the smooth quadric $Q$, then $Y$ is a complete intersection $Y = Q \cap H$ for some hypersurface $H \subset \mathbb{P}^n_k$. This follows because from the exact sequence:

$$
0 \to \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^r}(-2) \to \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^r} \to \mathcal{O}_Q \to 0

$$

we deduce that $H^0(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^r}(n)) \to H^0(\mathcal{O}_Q(n))$ is an isomorphism for all $n$ (using $H^1(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^r}(n-2)) = 0$ when $r \geq 3$). Since $[Y] = d \cdot [H \cap Q]$ in $\operatorname{Cl}(Q)$ for some $d$, and since the linear system $|dH \cap Q|$ comes from degree-$d$ hypersurfaces in $\mathbb{P}^r$, any irreducible codimension-1 subvariety is cut out by a single hypersurface. In the case $d = 1$, $Y$ is cut out by a hyperplane.

Furthermore, since $\operatorname{Cl}(C(Q)) = 0$ for the affine cone $C(Q)$ when $r \geq 4$, by Hartshorne II.6.2 we conclude that $k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]/(x_0^2 + \cdots + x_r^2)$ is a UFD.

### Example: Smooth cubic surface $X \subset \mathbb{P}^3$, $\operatorname{Cl}(X) \cong \mathbb{Z}^7$ {#ecag-0265}

**Statement:** Let $X \subset \mathbb{P}^3_k$ be a smooth cubic surface over an algebraically closed field $k$. Then $\operatorname{Cl}(X) \cong \mathbb{Z}^7$.

**Construction/Proof:**

**Step 1 (27 lines).** A classical result (Cayley--Salmon theorem) states that every smooth cubic surface over an algebraically closed field contains exactly 27 lines $\ell_1, \ldots, \ell_{27}$.

**Step 2 (Blow-up description).** Every smooth cubic surface $X$ is isomorphic to the blow-up of $\mathbb{P}^2_k$ at 6 points $p_1, \ldots, p_6$ in general position (no 3 collinear, no 6 on a conic). Let $\sigma : X \to \mathbb{P}^2$ be this blow-up. Then:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(X) \cong \operatorname{Pic}(\mathbb{P}^2) \oplus \bigoplus_{i=1}^{6} \mathbb{Z} \cdot [E_i] \cong \mathbb{Z}^7

$$

where $E_i = \sigma^{-1}(p_i)$ are the exceptional divisors and $\operatorname{Pic}(\mathbb{P}^2) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ is generated by the pullback $H = \sigma^* \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}(1)$.

**Step 3 (Generators).** A basis for $\operatorname{Pic}(X) \cong \mathbb{Z}^7$ is given by $\{H, E_1, \ldots, E_6\}$. The anticanonical class is $-K_X = 3H - E_1 - \cdots - E_6$, which corresponds to the hyperplane class of the cubic embedding $X \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^3$.

**Step 4 (Intersection form).** The intersection form on $\operatorname{Pic}(X)$ is determined by $H^2 = 1$, $E_i^2 = -1$, $H \cdot E_i = 0$, $E_i \cdot E_j = 0$ for $i \neq j$. This lattice is isomorphic to $I_{1,6} = \langle 1 \rangle \oplus \langle -1 \rangle^6$.

**Step 5 (The 27 lines in terms of $H, E_i$).** The 27 lines decompose as: 6 exceptional curves $E_i$; 15 strict transforms of lines $\overline{p_i p_j}$ through pairs of blown-up points, with class $H - E_i - E_j$; and 6 strict transforms of conics through 5 of the 6 points, with class $2H - E_1 - \cdots - E_6 + E_i$ (omitting $E_i$). Each of these 27 curves has self-intersection $-1$ and is a $(-1)$-curve.

**Step 6 (Smoothness of $X$).** Since $X$ is smooth and projective, $\operatorname{Cl}(X) \cong \operatorname{Pic}(X) \cong \mathbb{Z}^7$.

**Key Insight:** The blow-up description of cubic surfaces as $\mathbb{P}^2$ blown up at 6 points reduces the Picard group computation to the elementary fact that each blow-up adds one $\mathbb{Z}$-summand.

**Prerequisites:** Blow-up of surfaces, exceptional divisors, Cayley--Salmon theorem on 27 lines, cubic surfaces, intersection theory on surfaces

### Example: $\operatorname{Pic}(X)$ of flag varieties {#ecag-0266}

**Statement:** Let $F_n = SL_n / B_+$ be the complete flag variety, where $B_+$ is the Borel subgroup of upper triangular matrices in $SL_n$ over $\mathbb{C}$. Then:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(F_n) \cong \mathbb{Z}^{n-1}.

$$

**Construction/Proof:**

**Step 1.** The group $G = SL_n$ is connected, simply connected, and semisimple, so $\operatorname{Pic}(G) = 0$. We may apply Popov's exact sequence:

$$
0 \to \operatorname{X}_G(B_+) \to \operatorname{X}(B_+) \to \operatorname{Pic}(G/B_+) \to 0.

$$

**Step 2.** The character group $\operatorname{X}(B_+) \cong \operatorname{X}(T)$ where $T \subset B_+$ is the maximal torus of diagonal matrices, because any character of $B_+$ factors through $B_+ / [B_+, B_+] \cong T$. Since $T \cong (\mathbb{G}_m)^{n-1}$ (the constraint is $\det = 1$), we have $\operatorname{X}(B_+) \cong \mathbb{Z}^{n-1}$.

**Step 3.** Since $[SL_n, SL_n] = SL_n$, we have $\operatorname{X}(SL_n) = 0$. A character of $B_+$ extends to $SL_n$ if and only if it is trivial (there are no nontrivial characters of $SL_n$). Hence $\operatorname{X}_{SL_n}(B_+) = 0$.

**Step 4.** The exact sequence gives:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(F_n) \cong \operatorname{X}(B_+) / \operatorname{X}_{SL_n}(B_+) \cong \mathbb{Z}^{n-1}.

$$

The generators correspond to the fundamental weights $\omega_1, \ldots, \omega_{n-1}$, and the associated line bundles $\mathcal{L}(\omega_i)$ are the line bundles whose global sections give the Plucker embeddings.

**Key Insight:** The Picard group of the flag variety is identified with the weight lattice modulo the root lattice, which for $SL_n$ (simply connected) reduces to the full weight lattice of rank $n - 1$.

### Remark: $\operatorname{Pic}(G/B)$ {#ecag-0267}

By the Bruhat decomposition, the generalized flag variety $G/B$ (for a connected reductive group $G$ with Borel subgroup $B$) has a cell decomposition indexed by the Weyl group $W$. Each Schubert cell $C_w = BwB/B$ has codimension $\ell(w)$ in $G/B$, where $\ell(w)$ is the length of $w \in W$. The Schubert divisors (codimension-1 Schubert varieties) correspond to the simple reflections $s_\alpha$ for $\alpha$ in the set of simple roots $\Delta$.

The Picard group of $G/B$ is:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(G/B) \cong \mathbb{Z}^{|\Delta|}

$$

where $|\Delta|$ is the number of simple roots, equivalently the rank of $G$. Note that $|\Delta|$ equals the number of simple roots, **not** the number of positive roots $|\Phi_+|$.

More precisely, the generators of $\operatorname{Pic}(G/B)$ are the line bundles $\mathcal{L}(\omega_\alpha)$ associated to the fundamental weights $\omega_\alpha$ for $\alpha \in \Delta$. The corresponding Weil divisors are the Schubert divisors $\overline{B w_0 s_\alpha B / B}$, where $w_0$ is the longest element of the Weyl group. The Chow group $A^1(G/B) \cong \mathbb{Z}^{|\Delta|}$ is freely generated by the classes of these Schubert divisors.

### Remark: When do we have $\operatorname{X}(G) = 0$? {#ecag-0270}

The character group $\operatorname{X}(G) = \operatorname{Hom}(G, \mathbb{G}_m)$ of a connected linear algebraic group $G$ vanishes if and only if $G = [G, G]$, i.e., $G$ equals its own derived subgroup (commutator subgroup). This is encoded in the relationship between the character lattice and the weight lattice:

- For a connected reductive group $G$ with maximal torus $T$, the character group $\operatorname{X}(G)$ embeds into $\operatorname{X}(T)$ via restriction, and $\operatorname{X}(G) = 0$ if and only if the character lattice of $G$ equals the root lattice.
- In particular, $\operatorname{X}(G) = 0$ for any semisimple group $G$ (since $G = [G, G]$), and more specifically for simply connected semisimple groups like $SL_n$, $Sp_{2n}$, $\operatorname{Spin}(n)$.
- If $G$ is reductive but not semisimple, say $G = GL_n$, then $\operatorname{X}(G) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ (generated by the determinant). More generally, $\operatorname{X}(G) \cong \operatorname{X}(G / [G, G])$ where $G / [G, G]$ is a torus.

### Example: $\operatorname{Cl}(X)$ of Grassmannian {#ecag-0271}

**Statement:** Let $V$ be an $n$-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{C}$ and $1 \leq m \leq n - 1$. Then:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(\operatorname{Gr}(V, m)) \cong \mathbb{Z}.

$$

The generator is the determinant line bundle $\det(\mathcal{S}^*)$, where $\mathcal{S}$ is the tautological subbundle of rank $m$. Under the Plucker embedding, this generator corresponds to $\mathcal{O}(1)$.

**Construction/Proof:**

**Step 1.** The group $G = SL_n$ acts transitively on $\operatorname{Gr}(V, m)$. The stabilizer of the subspace $\langle e_1, \ldots, e_m \rangle$ is the parabolic subgroup:

$$
H = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ 0 & D \end{pmatrix} \in SL_n : A \in GL_m, D \in GL_{n-m} \right\}.

$$

So $\operatorname{Gr}(V, m) \cong SL_n / H$.

**Step 2.** Since $[SL_n, SL_n] = SL_n$, we have $\operatorname{X}(SL_n) = 0$ and $\operatorname{Pic}(SL_n) = 0$. By Popov's exact sequence:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(\operatorname{Gr}(V, m)) \cong \operatorname{X}(H) / \operatorname{X}_{SL_n}(H).

$$

Since $\operatorname{X}_{SL_n}(H) = 0$ (no nontrivial character of $H$ extends to $SL_n$), we get $\operatorname{Pic}(\operatorname{Gr}(V, m)) \cong \operatorname{X}(H)$.

**Step 3.** The character group of the parabolic $H$ is $\operatorname{X}(H) \cong \operatorname{X}(H / [H, H])$. The derived subgroup $[H, H]$ consists of matrices in $H$ with $\det(A) = 1$ and $\det(D) = 1$, subject to $\det(A)\det(D) = 1$. Hence $H / [H, H] \cong \mathbb{G}_m$ via the map $h \mapsto \det(A)$, giving $\operatorname{X}(H) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.

**Step 4.** Therefore:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(\operatorname{Gr}(V, m)) \cong \mathbb{Z}.

$$

The generator corresponds to the character $\det(A)$ of $H$, which gives the line bundle $\det(\mathcal{S}^*)$ on the Grassmannian.

**Key Insight:** The Picard group of the Grassmannian has rank 1 because the stabilizer parabolic has a one-dimensional abelianization, reflecting the fact that the only "degree of freedom" for line bundles comes from the determinant of the tautological subbundle.

### Example: Relations between $\operatorname{K}(X)$ and $\operatorname{Cl}(X)$ {#ecag-0273}

**Statement:** Let $X$ be a Noetherian scheme. There is a natural map $c_1 : \operatorname{Pic}(X) \to K_0(X)$ sending a line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ to its class $[\mathcal{L}] - [\mathcal{O}_X]$ in the Grothendieck group. When $X$ is a smooth variety over a field, there is a Chern character isomorphism $\operatorname{ch} : K_0(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \xrightarrow{\sim} \bigoplus_p A^p(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, and the first Chern class gives a map $c_1 : \operatorname{Pic}(X) \to A^1(X) = \operatorname{Cl}(X)$ that is an isomorphism (for smooth $X$, $\operatorname{Pic}(X) \cong \operatorname{Cl}(X)$).

**Construction/Proof:**

**Step 1.** The Grothendieck group $K_0(X)$ is the free abelian group on isomorphism classes of locally free sheaves (vector bundles) modulo the relation $[\mathscr{E}] = [\mathscr{E}'] + [\mathscr{E}'']$ for every short exact sequence $0 \to \mathscr{E}' \to \mathscr{E} \to \mathscr{E}'' \to 0$. The map $\operatorname{Pic}(X) \to K_0(X)$ sends $\mathcal{L} \mapsto [\mathcal{L}]$ and is a group homomorphism since $[\mathcal{L}_1 \otimes \mathcal{L}_2] = [\mathcal{L}_1] \cdot [\mathcal{L}_2]$ in the ring structure, and $[\mathcal{L}] - [\mathcal{O}_X]$ is additive.

**Step 2.** The determinant map $\det : K_0(X) \to \operatorname{Pic}(X)$ sends $[\mathscr{E}] \mapsto [\det(\mathscr{E})] = [\bigwedge^{\operatorname{rk} \mathscr{E}} \mathscr{E}]$. This is a left inverse to the inclusion, so $\operatorname{Pic}(X)$ is a direct summand of $K_0(X)$.

**Step 3.** For $X$ smooth, the first Chern class $c_1 : K_0(X) \to A^1(X)$ factors through $\operatorname{Pic}(X) \xrightarrow{\sim} A^1(X)$. On the other hand, the Chern character provides the isomorphism $K_0(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \cong \bigoplus A^p(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, showing that rationally, $K$-theory decomposes into Chow groups by codimension.

**Step 4 (Example: $\mathbb{P}^n$).** For $X = \mathbb{P}^n$, $K_0(\mathbb{P}^n) \cong \mathbb{Z}[h]/(h^{n+1})$ where $h = [\mathcal{O}(1)] - 1$, and $\operatorname{Cl}(\mathbb{P}^n) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ generated by a hyperplane. The first Chern class $c_1([\mathcal{O}(d)]) = d \cdot [H]$ recovers the degree.

**Key Insight:** The Picard group sits naturally inside the Grothendieck group as the rank-1 part, and the first Chern class map provides the bridge to the Chow group / divisor class group. For smooth varieties, the relationship is an isomorphism in codimension 1.

**Prerequisites:** Grothendieck group $K_0$, Chern character, Chow groups, determinant line bundle, smooth varieties

### Example: $\operatorname{Cl}(X)$ of connected algebraic groups {#ecag-0274}

**Statement:** Let $G$ be a connected linear algebraic group over an algebraically closed field $k$ with maximal torus $T$ and Borel subgroup $B$. Then:

1. If $G$ is semisimple and simply connected, then $\operatorname{Pic}(G) = 0$.
2. If $G$ is semisimple of adjoint type, then $\operatorname{Pic}(G) \cong \operatorname{coker}(C)$ where $C$ is the Cartan matrix of $G$.
3. In general, the Picard group fits into the exact sequence:

$$
0 \to \operatorname{X}(G) \to \operatorname{X}(T) \to \operatorname{Pic}(G/B) \to \operatorname{Pic}(G) \to 0.

$$

**Construction/Proof:**

**Step 1.** The exact sequence arises from the fibration $G \to G/B$ with fiber $B$. Since $B$ is solvable, $\operatorname{Pic}(B) = 0$ and the Leray spectral sequence degenerates to give the stated sequence.

**Step 2.** The map $\operatorname{X}(T) \to \operatorname{Pic}(G/B)$ sends a character $\chi \in \operatorname{X}(T)$ to the line bundle $\mathcal{L}(\chi) = G \times^B k_\chi$ on $G/B$.

**Step 3.** For $G$ simply connected semisimple, $\operatorname{X}(G) = 0$ and $\operatorname{X}(T) \to \operatorname{Pic}(G/B)$ is an isomorphism (the weight lattice equals the character lattice of $T$, and $\operatorname{Pic}(G/B)$ is isomorphic to the weight lattice). Hence $\operatorname{Pic}(G) = 0$.

**Step 4.** For $G$ adjoint, $\operatorname{X}(T)$ is the root lattice $\Lambda_R$ and $\operatorname{Pic}(G/B) \cong \Lambda_W$ (the weight lattice). The map $\Lambda_R \to \Lambda_W$ is given by expressing simple roots in terms of fundamental weights, which is exactly the Cartan matrix. Hence $\operatorname{Pic}(G) \cong \Lambda_W / C \cdot \Lambda_R$.

**Step 5 (Examples).**
- $G = SL_n$ (simply connected, type $A_{n-1}$): $\operatorname{Pic}(SL_n) = 0$.
- $G = PGL_n$ (adjoint, type $A_{n-1}$): $\operatorname{Pic}(PGL_n) \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ (the cokernel of the Cartan matrix of type $A_{n-1}$).
- $G = SO_{2n+1}$ (adjoint, type $B_n$): $\operatorname{Pic}(SO_{2n+1}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

**Key Insight:** The Picard group of a connected algebraic group is controlled by the failure of the character lattice of the maximal torus to surject onto the Picard group of the flag variety, which is measured by the weight-to-root lattice index.

**Prerequisites:** Root systems, Cartan matrices, weight and root lattices, simply connected and adjoint groups, Borel subgroups, flag varieties

### Example: $\operatorname{Pic}(X)$ of determinantal varieties $R_{m \times n, s}$ {#ecag-0275}

**Statement:** Let $R_{m \times n, s}$ denote the variety of $m \times n$ matrices over $\mathbb{C}$ with rank at most $s$, where $s \leq m \leq n$. Then:

- If $s = m$, then $R_{m \times n, m} \cong \mathbb{A}^{mn}$, so $\operatorname{Pic}(R_{m \times n, m}) = 0$.
- If $s < m$, then $\operatorname{Pic}(R_{m \times n, s}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.

**Construction/Proof:**

*Case $s = m$:* Every $m \times n$ matrix has rank at most $m$, so $R_{m \times n, m}$ is the full space of matrices, isomorphic to $\mathbb{A}^{mn}$, which has trivial Picard group.

*Case $s < m$:*

**Step 1 (Stratification).** The variety $R_{m \times n, s}$ is stratified by rank: $R_{m \times n, s-1}$ is a closed subvariety, and the complement $U_s = R_{m \times n, s} \setminus R_{m \times n, s-1}$ consists of matrices of rank exactly $s$.

**Step 2 (Transitivity).** The group $G = SL_m \times SL_n$ acts on $U_s$ by $(g, h) \cdot M = gMh^{-1}$. This action is transitive: any rank-$s$ matrix can be brought to the standard form

$$
E_s = \begin{pmatrix} I_{s \times s} & 0_{s \times (n-s)} \\ 0_{(m-s) \times s} & 0_{(m-s) \times (n-s)} \end{pmatrix}

$$

by row and column operations.

**Step 3 (Stabilizer).** The stabilizer $H \subset G$ of $E_s$ consists of pairs:

$$
H = \left\{ \left( \begin{pmatrix} A & C \\ 0 & D \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} A & 0 \\ F & K \end{pmatrix} \right) \in SL_m \times SL_n \right\}

$$

where $A$ is $s \times s$ invertible, $D$ is $(m-s) \times (m-s)$ invertible, $K$ is $(n-s) \times (n-s)$ invertible, with $\det(A)\det(D) = 1$ and $\det(A)\det(K) = 1$.

**Step 4 (Dimension).** From $\dim(U_s) = \dim(G) - \dim(H)$:

$$
\dim(R_{m \times n, s}) = (m^2 - 1) + (n^2 - 1) - [s^2 + (m-s)^2 - 1 + s(m-s) + (n-s)^2 + s(n-s)] = (m + n - s)s.

$$

**Step 5 (Codimension estimate).** We have:

$$
\dim(R_{m \times n, s}) - \dim(R_{m \times n, s-1}) = (m + n - s)s - (m + n - s + 1)(s - 1) = m + n - 2s + 1 \geq 3

$$

since $s < m \leq n$ implies $m + n - 2s + 1 \geq 3$. Therefore $R_{m \times n, s-1}$ has codimension $\geq 2$ in $R_{m \times n, s}$, and by the purity of the excision sequence, the restriction $\operatorname{Pic}(R_{m \times n, s}) \to \operatorname{Pic}(U_s)$ is an isomorphism.

**Step 6 (Character computation).** By Popov's theorem, $\operatorname{Pic}(U_s) \cong \operatorname{X}(H)$. The character group of $H$ is generated by the single character:

$$
\det_A : \left( \begin{pmatrix} A & C \\ 0 & D \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} A & 0 \\ F & K \end{pmatrix} \right) \mapsto \det(A).

$$

This character generates a free abelian group of rank 1, so $\operatorname{X}(H) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.

**Step 7 (Conclusion).**

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(R_{m \times n, s}) \cong \operatorname{Pic}(U_s) \cong \operatorname{X}(H) \cong \mathbb{Z}.

$$

**Key Insight:** The codimension of $R_{m \times n, s-1}$ inside $R_{m \times n, s}$ is always at least 3, so the Picard group is determined entirely by the open stratum $U_s$, which as a homogeneous space has Picard group equal to the character group of its stabilizer.

**Prerequisites:** Determinantal varieties, group actions on matrix spaces, Popov's exact sequence, excision for Picard groups, codimension estimates

### Remark {#ecag-0276}

The determinantal variety $R_{m \times n, s}$ has a natural stratification by rank:

$$
\{0\} = R_{m \times n, 0} \subset R_{m \times n, 1} \subset \cdots \subset R_{m \times n, s}.

$$

However, this stratification cannot be used directly with Kleiman's theorem (which computes Chow groups via cellular decompositions) because the individual strata $U_j = R_{m \times n, j} \setminus R_{m \times n, j-1}$ are not isomorphic to affine spaces $\mathbb{A}^N$. Instead, each stratum is a homogeneous space for $SL_m \times SL_n$, which is rationally but not cellularly decomposed. Nevertheless, the Picard group computation using codimension estimates and the Popov exact sequence yields the same answer ($\mathbb{Z}$) that one would formally obtain by a naive (and unjustified) application of Kleiman's theorem.

### Example: $\operatorname{Pic}(X)$ of the variety $S_{n,m}$ {#ecag-0277}

**Statement:** Let $S_{n,m}$ be the variety of unordered $m$-frames in an $n$-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{C}$ (i.e., the set of unordered $m$-tuples of linearly independent vectors, considered as a quotient by the symmetric group acting on the ordering). Then the Picard group is:

- If $m = 1$: $\operatorname{Pic}(S_{n,1}) = 0$ (since $S_{n,1} \cong \mathbb{A}^n \setminus \{0\}$, homotopy equivalent to $\mathbb{A}^n$).
- If $n = m = 1$ or $n = m = 2$: $\operatorname{Pic}(S_{n,n}) = 0$.
- If $n = m = 3$: $\operatorname{Pic}(S_{3,3}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$.
- If $n = m = 4$: $\operatorname{Pic}(S_{4,4}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$.
- If $n = m \geq 5$: $\operatorname{Pic}(S_{n,n}) = 0$.
- If $n > m > 1$: $\operatorname{Pic}(S_{n,m}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

**Construction/Proof:** The group $GL_n$ acts transitively on $S_{n,m}$, realized as a homogeneous space with canonical algebraic structure. The stabilizer $H$ of the frame $\{e_1, \ldots, e_m\}$ consists of matrices that permute $e_1, \ldots, e_m$ (and act invertibly on the complement). Since $\operatorname{Pic}(GL_n) = 0$ (as $GL_n$ is a connected reductive group with $[GL_n, GL_n] = SL_n$ and the only character is the determinant), Popov's theorem gives:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(S_{n,m}) \cong \operatorname{X}([GL_n, GL_n] \cap H) = \operatorname{X}(SL_n \cap H).

$$

Let $H' = H \cap SL_n$.

**Case $m = 1$:** The stabilizer is $H \cong GL_{n-1}$ (matrices fixing $e_1$ up to scalar, acting freely on the complement). Then $H' = SL_{n-1}$, and $\operatorname{X}(SL_{n-1}) = 0$. Hence $\operatorname{Pic}(S_{n,1}) = 0$. Geometrically, $S_{n,1} \cong \mathbb{A}^n \setminus \{0\}$, which has trivial Picard group.

**Case $n = m$:** Here $H \cong S_n$ (the symmetric group, permuting the basis vectors). Then $H' = H \cap SL_n = A_n$ (the alternating group, permutations of even sign).

- If $n = 1$ or $2$: $A_1$ and $A_2$ are trivial, so $\operatorname{X}(A_n) = 0$ and $\operatorname{Pic}(S_{n,n}) = 0$.
- If $n = 3$: $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$, so $\operatorname{X}(A_3) \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ and $\operatorname{Pic}(S_{3,3}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$.
- If $n = 4$: $A_4$ is not abelian, but $[A_4, A_4]$ is the Klein four-group $V_4 \cong (\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z})^2$. Hence $A_4 / [A_4, A_4] \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$, giving $\operatorname{X}(A_4) \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ and $\operatorname{Pic}(S_{4,4}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$.
- If $n \geq 5$: $A_n$ is simple, so $[A_n, A_n] = A_n$ and $\operatorname{X}(A_n) = 0$. Hence $\operatorname{Pic}(S_{n,n}) = 0$.

**Case $n > m > 1$:** The stabilizer $H$ consists of block matrices:

$$
H = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} S & * \\ 0 & M \end{pmatrix} : S \in S_m \text{ (permutation matrices)}, M \in GL_{n-m} \right\}.

$$

Setting $H' = H \cap SL_n$ and computing:

$$
[H', H'] = \left\{ \begin{pmatrix} A_m & * \\ 0 & SL_{n-m} \end{pmatrix} \right\}

$$

(where $A_m$ denotes alternating permutation matrices). The quotient $H' / [H', H']$ has order 2: the only nontrivial character detects whether the permutation part has even or odd sign (modulo the determinant-1 constraint). Hence:

$$
\operatorname{Pic}(S_{n,m}) \cong \operatorname{X}(H' / [H', H']) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}.

$$

**Key Insight:** The Picard group of the variety of unordered frames is determined by the abelianization of the alternating group (when $n = m$) or a related derived subgroup quotient (when $n > m$), connecting the algebraic geometry of homogeneous spaces to the representation theory of symmetric and alternating groups.

**Prerequisites:** Symmetric and alternating groups, abelianization, character groups of finite groups, Popov's exact sequence, homogeneous spaces of $GL_n$
